Creative Australia
Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications, Sport & Arts · Corporate Plan 2025-29 · BD confidence: Medium

Creative Australia is the Commonwealth's expanded arts investment and development agency (formerly the Australia Council), reshaped under the Revive National Cultural Policy and the Creative Australia Act 2023. It is a grant-dominated corporate entity — c.$267m of its ~$318m expense base is grants — undergoing intensive organisational transformation to stand up new functions (First Nations First, Writing Australia, Creative Workplaces, the Creative Futures Fund) while admitting workforce strain, cyber/AI risk and a need to mature governance and risk. The 2025 Venice Biennale crisis, an independent governance review with nine recommendations, an ANAO fraud-and-corruption-control audit and Senate Estimates scrutiny make governance, risk, assurance and change the sharpest near-term demand signals. As a smaller, custodial institution with modest own-source revenue, opportunities should be right-sized to advisory, framework-design, evaluation and assurance work rather than large delivery programs.

Strategic priorities

  • Embed First Nations self-determination (First Nations First) — Finalise and begin implementing the First Nations Arts and Culture Strategic Plan 2026-30, expand First Nations peer/advisor panels, deliver the remit of the new First Nations Board and First Nations Arts unit, and develop a First Nations creative workforce strategy.
  • Implement the Revive expanded remit — Stand up and bed down new functions and an expanded investment portfolio under Revive — including Writing Australia, the Creative Futures Fund and Creative Workplaces — coherently across new and existing programs.
  • Sustainable creative careers and IP protection — Build skills/talent pipelines, deliver the Fair, Safe and Respectful Creative Workplaces Framework and a national workplaces survey baseline, and support artists to protect creative IP amid GenAI threats.
  • Grow philanthropic and private investment — Maximise impact by facilitating private giving (target $11m), building fundraising capability across the sector, and combining philanthropic, commercial and public funding.
  • Research, evaluation and influence — Use research, insights and evaluation to influence whole-of-government policy, build the evidence base for the value of arts and culture, and report against new and largely qualitative performance measures.
  • Technology, data and cyber capability uplift — Improve cloud platforms, develop a robust cyber security framework aligned to government policy, manage cyber supply-chain and AI risk, and extend data-sharing (Data Hubs) and analytics for funded organisations.

Reform & change drivers

  • Revive National Cultural Policy and the Creative Australia Act 2023 driving an expanded, modernised remit and new statutory functions
  • Establishment of new bodies/functions: First Nations First and First Nations Board (2024), Writing Australia, Creative Workplaces, the Creative Futures Fund
  • Proliferation of generative AI raising copyright, consent and remuneration concerns for artists, requiring policy and IP responses
  • Cost-of-living and operating-cost pressures (printing, insurance, freight, touring) eroding sector revenue streams
  • Workforce and skills shortages in the creative sector not replenished since the pandemic
  • New and largely qualitative PGPA performance measures introduced alongside the new objectives
  • Net zero by 2030 commitment and Environmental Action Plan obligations

Challenges & risks

  • Risk that the Revive expanded remit fails to deliver a coherent, efficient investment portfolio across new and existing programs
  • Sustained strain on staff and workload during the intensive implementation period impacting service delivery
  • Achievement of legislated functions impacted by increasing demand (quantum and diversity) against limited resources
  • IT infrastructure compromised through unauthorised access to data and information assets (cyber)
  • Reputational risk that Creative Australia is perceived as not serving diverse stakeholders — acutely realised in the 2025 Venice Biennale crisis
  • Financial sustainability: thin own-source revenue (falling from ~$17m to ~$6m), grant-timing deficits, and future surpluses fully offset by office-lease cash losses
  • Newly introduced, largely qualitative performance measures that may be hard to evidence and report against
  • Fraud and corruption control adequacy across investment/funding programs (subject of an ANAO audit)

In the news & controversies

  • In February 2025 Creative Australia's board rescinded its appointment of artist Khaled Sabsabi and curator Michael Dagostino as Australia's 2026 Venice Biennale representatives days after announcing them, following media and political backlash over earlier works, triggering a major reputational crisis. (The Conversation — https://theconversation.com/creative-australias-backflip-on-venice-biennale-representatives-exposes-deep-governance-failures-260402 ; ARTnews — https://www.artnews.com/art-news/news/khaled-sabsabi-australian-pavilion-venice-biennale-controversy-resignations-creative-australia-1234732471/)
    Acute, current demand for crisis management, board governance reform, risk-assessment frameworks and reputation rebuilding — a clear advisory entry point.
  • The reversal prompted resignations of senior staff (Head of Visual Arts Mikala Tai, program manager Tahmina Maskinyar), the departure of board member Lindy Lee, and the withdrawal of major donor and former Venice commissioner Simon Mordant. (The Conversation — https://theconversation.com/creative-australias-backflip-on-venice-biennale-representatives-exposes-deep-governance-failures-260402)
    Loss of internal capability and philanthropic relationships creates openings for interim resourcing, donor/stakeholder re-engagement strategy and program continuity support.
  • An independent review by Blackhall & Pearl (released ~July 2025) found systemic governance failings — no formal risk-assessment process, no crisis plan, no escalation mechanism — found no political interference, and made nine recommendations including stronger governance frameworks and board training. (The Conversation — https://theconversation.com/creative-australias-backflip-on-venice-biennale-representatives-exposes-deep-governance-failures-260402 ; Artsy — https://www.artsy.net/article/artsy-editorial-khaled-sabsabi-reselected-represent-australia-2026-venice-biennale-controversy)
    A documented nine-recommendation remediation list is a concrete pipeline for governance, risk-framework and board-capability implementation work.
  • On 2 July 2025 the board reinstated Sabsabi and Dagostino after the review, with Wesley Enoch as acting chair and Mordant reappointed as global ambassador; chair Robert Morgan retired in May 2025 while CEO Adrian Collette remained and apologised. (Global Voices Advox — https://advox.globalvoices.org/2025/07/23/creative-australia-restores-representatives-for-the-2026-venice-biennale-in-a-win-for-freedom-of-expression/ ; Artsy — https://www.artsy.net/article/artsy-editorial-khaled-sabsabi-reselected-represent-australia-2026-venice-biennale-controversy)
