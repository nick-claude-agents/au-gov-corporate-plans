Australian Institute of Criminology (AIC) — Business Development Brief

Australian Institute of Criminology (AIC)
Home Affairs · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (four-year outlook to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Medium

The AIC is a small, expertise-heavy research and knowledge agency on crime and justice within the Home Affairs portfolio — a scientific/research archetype with a custodial data role. It is statutorily independent but heavily dependent on the ACIC, which provides all corporate services, ICT (shared services), office space and employs all AIC staff. The plan signals two clear practical workstreams this year: a move of IT infrastructure to a cloud environment and a redesign of the Indigenous Justice Clearinghouse website (folded into the main AIC site). It also flags a named enterprise risk around transitioning the AIC's independence, alongside data-management/cyber and finance/procurement risks. Demand is modest in dollar terms and constrained by the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework (external support only in 'limited/exceptional circumstances'), so opportunities are best-fit, proportionate and likely small advisory or digital pieces rather than large delivery programs.

Strategic priorities

  • Deliver policy-relevant crime and justice research on national priorities — Core function: undertake impartial research across seven priorities (transnational serious and organised crime, cybercrime, violent extremism, domestic/family/sexual violence, online child sexual exploitation, human trafficking/modern slavery, Indigenous over-representation) plus six statistical monitoring collections.
  • Administer the Criminology Research Grants and awards — Run an effective annual grants program funding external (university) researchers on four priority themes, plus the Australian Crime and Violence Prevention Awards, and disseminate findings.
  • Move IT infrastructure to a cloud environment — In 2025-26 the AIC will migrate its IT infrastructure to cloud (via ACIC shared services) for greater future flexibility.
  • Redesign the Indigenous Justice Clearinghouse website — The Indigenous Justice Clearinghouse will be redesigned and incorporated into the main AIC website, with a web designer to be engaged.
  • Transition and mature AIC independence — A named enterprise risk concerns effectively transitioning the AIC's independence to meet future purpose and objectives, alongside a new AIC-specific risk framework introduced in 2024-25.

Reform & change drivers

  • New AIC-specific Risk Management Framework introduced in 2024-25, moving away from the ACIC framework to better align with AIC objectives
  • Named 'Transition' enterprise risk around effecting AIC independence to meet future purpose — implies an evolving operating-model relationship with ACIC
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework constraining external/consultant use to limited and exceptional circumstances
  • Cloud migration of IT infrastructure in 2025-26
  • Evolving and emerging crime problems (cybercrime, online exploitation, modern slavery) reshaping the research program
  • Restricted access to state/territory crime and justice data requiring negotiated data-sharing arrangements

Challenges & risks

  • Heavy dependence on ACIC for corporate services, ICT, office space and as the legal employer of all AIC staff — a concentration and operating-model risk
  • Data enterprise risk explicitly covering storage and destruction of physical/digital data including cyber security
  • Finance and resources risk covering financial management, contracts, procurement and fraud prevention/detection
  • 'Transition' risk around securing/maturing the AIC's independence
  • Ethics and research-integrity risk (unbiased research, staff integrity) in a low-risk-appetite, reputation-sensitive environment
  • Small specialist workforce with early-career recruitment model — capability concentration and key-person dependency
  • Qualitative/vague performance measures (e.g. 'qualitative case studies' for policy impact) that may understate demonstrable outcomes

Recommended actions

  • High Approach the AIC's communications/information services lead (and the ACIC shared-services ICT function) with a tight UX-and-build capability statement for the Indigenous Justice Clearinghouse redesign.
    This is the single most concrete, externally-sanctioned 2025-26 deliverable; the plan already names engaging a web designer, so the procurement window is near-term and the fit is exact.
  • Medium Engage the ACIC shared-services/ICT function and the AIC Deputy Director on cloud migration and associated data/cyber assurance, positioning a proportionate migration and security-assessment offer.
    Cloud move is named for 2025-26 and intersects the Data enterprise risk; early conversation clarifies whether spend sits with AIC or ACIC and how to be positioned.
  • Medium Monitor for any AIC or ACIC approaches to market on web/cloud services and any future review of AIC independence, given the joint AIC-ACIC Audit and Risk Committee oversight.
    Procurement signals for the named digital deliverables may surface via ACIC's shared-services channels; watching avoids missing a small but winnable scope.
  • Low Prepare a concise operating-model/governance thought piece on maturing statutory independence within a shared-services arrangement, and offer it to the Director/Deputy Director.
    The 'Transition' enterprise risk is named but undefined; a credible, low-commitment thought piece tests appetite without over-investing in a speculative opportunity.

Who to approach

Director and Deputy Director, AIC (accountable authority and enterprise risk owner), AIC Communications and Information Services lead (owner of website and Clearinghouse redesign), ACIC shared-services / ICT function (delivers AIC's cloud migration and corporate ICT), Joint AIC and ACIC Audit and Risk Committee (financial/performance reporting, risk and internal controls), ACIC procurement/corporate services (legal employer and procurement pathway for AIC needs)
