Office of the Fair Work Ombudsman (FWO) — Business Development Brief

Generated 05 June 2026 · Source corporate plan
Office of the Fair Work Ombudsman (FWO)
Employment and Workplace Relations · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

The FWO is a ~1,000-person workplace regulator (regulator archetype) with a 2025-26 operating budget of $179.6m, rising to $194.5m by 2028-29. Its dominant story this year is becoming a dual civil-and-criminal regulator: from 1 January 2025 intentional wage underpayment is a criminal offence and the FWO has stood up a new Criminal Investigations Branch, new operating procedures, and supporting technology and physical infrastructure. It is simultaneously building 'strategic enforcement' (data-driven prioritisation), enhancing data/analytics and predictive capability, and responding to an ANAO performance audit that found risk-management and regulatory-oversight maturity needs improvement. KPI 5 ('serious and/or systemic non-compliance is detected and addressed') remains under development with an external provider engaged, signalling an open performance-measurement and evidence-base need. The combination of a high-profile new criminal jurisdiction under parliamentary scrutiny, an ANAO-flagged risk uplift, data ambitions and a new People Strategy creates several credible, time-bound advisory opportunities — tempered by the agency's in-house-first Strategic Commissioning Framework posture.

Strategic priorities

  • Operationalise the new criminal wage-underpayment jurisdiction — Embed the new Criminal Investigations Branch and end-to-end processes for investigating the new intentional-underpayment Offence and referring matters to the CDPP/AFP, while educating the community and administering safe-harbour mechanisms (Voluntary Small Business Wage Compliance Code and Cooperation Agreements).
  • Strategic enforcement and data-driven prioritisation — Implement the new Compliance and Enforcement Policy built on prioritisation, deterrence, sustainability and system-wide effect, using data analytics, predictive analytics and behavioural economics to target limited enforcement resources for maximum impact.
  • Strengthen risk management and regulatory oversight (ANAO response) — Mature the Risk Management Framework, refresh the strategic risk register, and improve governance support for a risk-based approach, explicitly in response to the ANAO audit of the FWO's regulatory functions.
  • Develop a measure of regulatory effectiveness (KPI 5) — Build a meaningful performance measure for detecting and addressing serious and/or systemic non-compliance, including enhancing the Regulatory Compliance Risk Model and scoping a survey of compliance rates across the regulated economy.
  • Technology consolidation, data value and cyber resilience — Deliver the Technology Strategy and Data Strategy: consolidate and simplify the technology landscape, safeguard core systems against cyber threats, explore AI, and derive value from data while integrating with whole-of-government digital transformation.
  • Workforce capability and the new People Strategy — Launch the FWO People Strategy and build regulatory/investigative capability (Certificate IV and new Diploma in Government Investigations, SES Performance Leadership Framework) to staff the dual jurisdiction and address psychosocial risk.

Reform & change drivers

  • Fair Work Legislation Amendment (Closing Loopholes) Act 2023 introducing the criminal Offence for intentional underpayment from 1 January 2025, creating a dual civil/criminal jurisdiction.
  • New Compliance and Enforcement Policy (effective 1 January 2025) based on strategic-enforcement principles.
  • Minister's Statement of Expectations (13 December 2024) and the FWO's Statement of Intent (January 2025) refreshing expectations on regulatory effectiveness and continuous improvement.
  • ANAO performance audit (Report No. 30 of 2024-25) flagging risk-management and regulatory-oversight maturity gaps.
  • Establishment of a standing tripartite Advisory Group (peak employers and ACTU) plus sector reference groups requiring co-design and stakeholder engagement.
  • Whole-of-government digital transformation agenda and cyber-security expectations.
  • APS reform, Strategic Commissioning Framework (core work kept in-house), and the SES Performance Leadership Framework.
  • Budget growth to $194.5m by 2028-29 subject to efficiency dividends, funding new activities and technology investment.

Challenges & risks

  • Standing up criminal-standard investigation capability, evidence handling and CDPP/AFP referral processes under intense external scrutiny over the absence of prosecutions.
  • ANAO-identified deficiency in strategic oversight of resourcing and impacts when monitoring annual regulatory priorities.
  • KPI 5 (regulatory effectiveness) remains undeveloped, leaving a gap in demonstrating impact of serious/systemic non-compliance work.
  • Risk of regulatory capture and the need for impartial, accountable decision-making (named strategic risk).
  • Data and information risk: generating, governing, protecting and deriving value from data (named strategic risk).
  • Technology risk: failing to create and maintain fit-for-purpose technology amid consolidation and AI exploration (named strategic risk).
  • Workforce capability and retention risk in a changing environment, plus psychosocial hazard management.
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework limits outsourcing of core regulatory work, constraining the scope of external advisory engagements to enabling/corporate functions.

In the news & controversies

  • Intentional wage underpayment became a criminal offence on 1 January 2025, but reporting in 2026 notes no prosecutions had yet occurred, prompting two inquiries to seek explanations from the FWO as the responsible regulator/referral body. (The Conversation — https://theconversation.com/no-one-has-been-prosecuted-for-wage-theft-since-it-became-a-crime-2-inquiries-want-answers-280578)

  • The FWO issued a public statement on the CFMEU in July 2024 amid the broader union-misconduct controversy, placing its enforcement role in the construction sector under heightened attention. (Office of the Fair Work Ombudsman — https://www.fairwork.gov.au/newsroom/media-releases/2024-media-releases/july-2024/20240718/FWO-statement-on-CFMEU-media-release)
    Potential surge demand around enforcement strategy, intelligence and casework support in a high-profile, politically charged sector.

ANAO audit findings

  • ANAO Auditor-General Report No. 30 of 2024-25 found the OFWO largely effective in exercising its regulatory functions but identified a strategic-oversight gap — its understanding of resourcing and impacts when monitoring annual regulatory priorities was deficient. Three recommendations were made and all agreed. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/effectiveness-of-the-office-of-the-fair-work-ombudsmans-regulatory-functions)
    A clear, recommendation-led work pipeline for designing/uplifting regulatory-priority monitoring, resourcing-and-impact measurement, and tracking implementation of agreed recommendations.
  • An earlier ANAO audit (Report No. 14, 2012-13) found the FWO did not apply a structured risk-management approach to identifying/responding to key workplace-relations issues — a theme echoed in the 2024-25 audit's maturity gaps. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/delivery-workplace-relations-services-the-office-the-fair-work-ombudsman)
    Risk-management framework maturity has been a recurring ANAO concern, suggesting durable demand for enterprise risk and assurance uplift.
