National Museum of Australia (NMA)
Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications, Sport & Arts · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-26 to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Medium

The NMA is a corporate Commonwealth collecting institution operating on a constrained budget supplemented by the 2023-24 $78.3m National Collecting Institutions funding package, with commercial and philanthropic revenue an explicit growth ambition. The plan's strongest, most time-bound signals are operational: a digital uplift that will procure and implement a Trusted Digital Repository and a new Content Management System to replace ageing infrastructure, delivery of a Strategic Asset Management Plan against an end-of-life HVAC and critical building systems, and financial-sustainability pressure under cost-of-living and global economic uncertainty. Multi-year capital ambitions (Acton site precinct planning, Landmarks gallery refresh, First Australians gallery redevelopment for which it will 'seek additional funding') create a pipeline of program and assurance work. Note a headwind: under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework the Museum is actively reducing outsourcing of core work in Engineering/Technical and Service Delivery job families, so offerings must target genuinely specialist, project-shaped need and be right-sized to a small institution.

Strategic priorities

  • Digital and technology transformation — Replace ageing digital infrastructure and uplift digital capability by procuring and implementing a Trusted Digital Repository (TDR) and a new Content Management System (CMS), upgrading legacy systems, defining a cloud/system-ownership approach and maintaining cybersecurity maturity.
  • Asset management and critical infrastructure — Deliver a Strategic Asset Management Plan (SAMP) to assess future asset and funding needs, and progress urgent replacement of end-of-life plant including the HVAC system, building management and fire-safety systems.
  • Funding and own-source revenue growth — Grow commercial operations (retail, venue hire, tourism, e-commerce, ticketing) and philanthropy to build a sustainable revenue base, while managing cost pressure and uncertain visitation.
  • Acton site and gallery redevelopment — Undertake strategic planning for the Acton Peninsula precinct, refresh the Landmarks gallery, and progress the multi-year First Australians gallery redevelopment (subject to additional funding).
  • First Nations engagement and capability — Stand up the new First Nations division, develop a Cultural Plan, renew the Reconciliation Action Plan, deliver cultural-capability programs and grow First Nations workforce representation.
  • Touring program reset — Implement a new modular touring model commencing 2025, informed by a domestic-venue survey, to expand regional and metropolitan access to the collection.

Reform & change drivers

  • National Cultural Policy (Revive) and the Minister for the Arts' Statement of Expectations shaping strategic priorities
  • 2023-24 Budget funding package of $78.3m over four years for National Collecting Institutions, tied to effective resource management and transparent reporting
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework driving insourcing of core work and FTE/expenditure reduction targets in Engineering/Technical and Service Delivery job families
  • APS Net Zero 2030 emissions target and the Museum's Environmental Sustainability Action Plan (HVAC replacement, solar, gas removal, EV fleet)
  • Organisational realignment creating a new First Nations division
  • Growth in digital engagement (2.7m in 2018-19 to 7.4m in 2024-25) against ageing digital infrastructure
  • Cost-of-living and global economic uncertainty pressuring visitation and own-source revenue

Challenges & risks

  • Funding and financial sustainability: insufficient funds to pursue all strategic objectives; reliance on own-source and philanthropic revenue
  • End-of-life critical infrastructure (HVAC, building management, fire safety) requiring major works beyond existing capital funding
  • Ageing ICT/digital infrastructure and legacy systems needing modernisation and a defined cloud approach
  • IT and cybersecurity risk - maintaining Essential Eight maturity against unauthorised access and disruption
  • Deterioration/loss of National Historical Collection objects and storage capacity constraints
  • Critical skills and capability gaps in a workforce expected to deliver expanding strategic priorities while reducing outsourcing
  • Visitation and revenue volatility dependent on blockbuster international exhibitions
