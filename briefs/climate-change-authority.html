Climate Change Authority
Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-26 to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Low

The Climate Change Authority (CCA) is a small, expertise-heavy statutory advisory body (a non-corporate Commonwealth entity) with a 2025-26 operating budget of just $13.8m and ~62 staff. Its work is dominated by legislated outputs — the 2035 Targets Advice, the annual progress report, and the statutory 2026 review of the ACCU Scheme — and it explicitly commits, under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework, to NOT outsource its core advisory work. That materially limits the addressable market: genuine BD demand sits in the enabling/corporate layer, not in policy. The most credible openings are the newly implemented (and hard-to-measure) impact KPIs, the post-restructure organisational change agenda (where the plan explicitly contemplates external consultants), a stepped-up public engagement and communications ambition (amplified by a possible COP31 hosting role), and workforce/wellbeing/capability uplift. Engagements must be right-sized to a very small budget — short advisory and framework pieces, not multi-year delivery.

Strategic priorities

  • Deliver legislated advice and reviews — Complete the 2035 Targets Advice, deliver the fourth annual progress report informing the Minister's annual climate statement, and commence the statutory 2026 review of the ACCU Scheme (due to the Minister by 31 December 2026).
  • Step up community and stakeholder engagement — Broaden public engagement, host 'snap seminars', increase media and social cut-through, and support Australia's role at COP30/COP31, including a potential prominent coordinating role if Australia wins COP31 hosting rights.
  • Apply a fully revised performance framework — For the first time apply the full suite of 8 revised KPIs (mix of 'delivery' and 'impact' measures) to assess effectiveness, including hard-to-measure impact indicators such as policy translation and public perception.
  • Embed organisational change after restructure — Bed down a 2024-25 internal restructure that created a new outreach/enabling branch and a Deputy CEO role, optimise internal systems and embed stewardship practices.
  • Maintain a capable workforce and inclusive culture — Sustain capability after a period of restoration and growth, run a wellbeing 'pulse' survey, act on APS Census results, and progress a new 'Innovate' Reconciliation Action Plan.

Reform & change drivers

  • Major legislated deliverables in 2025-26: the 2035 Targets Advice, the annual progress report, and commencement of the statutory 2026 ACCU Scheme review.
  • Possible Australia/Pacific COP31 hosting in 2026, anticipated to give the Authority a 'prominent role' and coordination demands.
  • Building on recent Government climate policy foundations (Safeguard Mechanism reforms, Capacity Investment Scheme, New Vehicle Efficiency Standard, Future Made in Australia, mandatory climate-related financial disclosures).
  • Forthcoming National Climate Risk Assessment and National Adaptation Plan reshaping the adaptation debate.
  • Full implementation of a revised 8-KPI performance framework, including new 'impact' measures.
  • Post-restructure embedding (new outreach/enabling branch, new Deputy CEO role).
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework commitment to keep core work in-house, limiting outsourcing.

Challenges & risks

  • Financial sustainability named as a strategic risk for a $13.8m entity reliant on appropriations and shared services; the plan notes pursuing funding increases with the Portfolio Department.
  • People capability risk — ability to attract, develop and retain specialist staff in a small workforce.
  • Organisational change risk — explicitly contemplates engaging external consultants for roles/responsibilities and change management.
  • Information integrity/security risk, though ICT and cyber are provided by DCCEEW.
  • Reputational/'confidence and trust' risk given politically contested target-setting advice.
  • New impact KPIs (e.g. policy translation, public perception) are inherently difficult to measure robustly.
  • Reliance on other agencies (DCCEEW, DISR) for ICT, cyber, finance/payroll and office space constrains in-house control.
