National Library of Australia (NLA) — Business Development Brief

Generated 31 May 2026 · Source corporate plan
National Library of Australia (NLA)
Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications and the Arts · Corporate Plan 2024-25 · BD confidence: Medium

The NLA is a corporate Commonwealth entity and cultural/collecting institution stewarding one of the world's largest collections, with its national digital platform Trove at the centre of its mission. This year's plan is dominated by infrastructure modernisation: a five-year roadmap to replace Trove's end-of-life software (including its metadata engine), cyber-security uplift, major heritage-building works on the Parkes building, and an extension of the Hume Repository to address long-term collection storage. The plan openly flags funding-sustainability risk, end-of-life core IT systems, labour-market competition for specialist skills, and new (baseline-to-be-set) performance measures. Notably, the NLA is reducing outsourcing under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework, which is a headwind for advisory spend, so opportunities should be proportionate to a modest budget and concentrated where the Library lacks internal capacity (digital build, costing/business cases, assurance). ANAO follow-on audit findings (storage strategy, deselection/disposal policy, business continuity, LMS security) and a reported staff underpayment add concrete, demand-creating pressures.

Strategic priorities

  • Modernise and stabilise Trove — Execute a five-year implementation roadmap to replace Trove's end-of-life software, stabilise and secure the platform, and replace the underlying metadata engine to improve efficiency and capacity.
  • Major building works and long-term collection storage — Progress major works and preventative maintenance on the heritage-listed Parkes building, construct the Hume Repository extension, and plan for longer-term storage needs while remaining open to the public.
  • Digital, data and cyber capability uplift — Execute the Digital Strategy to ensure suitable digital infrastructure, uplift cyber security, adopt cloud/SaaS in line with government priorities, deliver a more accessible website and collection search, and operationalise handwritten text recognition/transcription.
  • Funding sustainability and demonstrating return on investment — Secure a sustainable funding and resource model to meet the institutional mandate, maintain the heritage building, and sustain Trove to world-leading standards; continue to advocate for future funding needs to government.
  • Reshape workforce and reduce reliance on external labour — Strengthen capability via the Strategic People Pathway, reduce outsourcing of core (initially information/knowledge management) work under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework, and embed the new Enterprise Agreement.
  • Refresh the performance framework — Introduce new effectiveness measures (digital and onsite visitor satisfaction surveys with baselines to be set) and retire several output/service-charter measures to improve the 'clear read' across PBS, Corporate Plan and Annual Report.

Reform & change drivers

  • National Cultural Policy (Revive) alignment shaping collecting, engagement and infrastructure priorities
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework requiring reduced outsourcing of core work
  • Generative AI rapidly altering the operating context for a custodian of large-scale digital content
  • Government cloud/SaaS priorities driving migration of enterprise services
  • 2023 funding commitment creating public expectation that building and service upgrades will be delivered
  • New Enterprise Agreement (effective April 2024) requiring refresh of supporting policies
  • Archives Act 1983 information-governance obligations
  • Physical collection growth of ~2.5km per year intensifying storage pressure

Challenges & risks

  • Funding/resource model may be insufficient to sustain the mandate, building and Trove over the medium-long term
  • Core IT systems at end of life; rising digital infrastructure, supplier, project and contractor costs
  • Increasing cyber-security threats to systems and data, against a stated low risk appetite for confidentiality/integrity/availability
  • Labour-market competition for specialist skills and reliance on hard-to-retain expertise
  • Managing multiple building projects within a functioning, heritage-listed public facility
  • Long-term collection storage unresolved as current arrangements approach end of life
  • New satisfaction measures lack baselines, leaving effectiveness reporting immature in 2024-25

ANAO audit findings

  • Auditor-General Report No. 29 of 2020-21 'Management of the National Collections — Follow-on' found NLA governance and collection management 'largely effective' but flagged no plan for removing items from collections, unresolved long-term storage, preservation recording not integrated with the collection-management system, and sub-standard library-management-system security. Two recommendations to the NLA (update business continuity plan; develop a deselection/disposal policy) were agreed. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/management-the-national-collections-follow)
    Audit-driven, agreed recommendations (deselection/disposal policy, business continuity, storage strategy, system security) are ready-made, defensible workstreams for assurance, governance and digital remediation support.
