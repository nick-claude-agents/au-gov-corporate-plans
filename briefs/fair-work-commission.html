Fair Work Commission
Employment and Workplace Relations · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

The Fair Work Commission is Australia's national workplace relations tribunal — a small, expertise-heavy regulatory/quasi-judicial entity that resolves disputes, sets minimum conditions and oversees registered organisations. After a significant expansion of functions over the last three years (Secure Jobs/Better Pay and related reforms), the 2025-26 plan is explicitly about consolidating: refining and evolving service delivery, streamlining processes, improving access for all parties, and strengthening education, outreach and self-help resources. External signals are far sharper than the published plan: the President has publicly described an 'unsustainable' caseload (44,075 lodgements, ~75% growth over three years) colliding with an ~11% budget reduction and AI-inflated application volumes. That workload-versus-funding crunch is the dominant, self-declared demand signal — pointing to operating-model, case-management, digital/AI and efficiency work — tempered by an entity in budget-repair mode and politically sensitive about perceptions of impartiality.

Strategic priorities

  • Consolidate after function expansion — Refine and evolve how the Commission delivers services following a significant expansion of functions over the last three years, embedding new responsibilities into sustainable business-as-usual.
  • Streamline processes and improve access — Enhance how the community accesses Commission services by streamlining processes and improving access for all parties to proceedings.
  • Education, outreach and self-help resources — Support education and outreach programs and build on the established suite of resources so parties can navigate the system with less hands-on intervention.

Reform & change drivers

  • Significant expansion of functions over three years (bargaining and workplace-relations reforms) now requiring operational consolidation
  • Caseload growth described by the President as 'unsustainable' — 44,075 lodgements in 2024-25, ~10% year-on-year and ~75% over three years
  • Budget reduction of ~11% with an actual ~$2m cut in-year and funding not returning to prior levels until 2028-29
  • AI being used by applicants (>40%) inflating application volumes and changing the nature of filings
  • Growing presence of unregulated 'paid agents' in proceedings, prompting a dedicated working group
  • Ongoing implementation scrutiny of multi-employer bargaining reforms

Challenges & risks

  • Demand outstripping operational, performance and funding structures simultaneously
  • Doing more with less: efficiency targets while caseload rises
  • Quality and integrity risk from unregulated paid agents appearing before the tribunal
  • AI-driven volume and quality issues in lodgements
  • Reputational risk around perceptions of partiality tied to member appointments (criticised by both employer groups and unions)
  • Constrained discretionary spend limits appetite for large advisory engagements

In the news & controversies

  • FWC President Justice Adam Hatcher publicly warned that application growth is 'unsustainable within the Commission's current operational, performance and funding structures', with 44,075 lodgements in 2024/25 — ~10% up year-on-year and ~24% above the five-year average, driven by general protections and unfair dismissal claims. (Gadens legal insight, 3 Dec 2025 — https://www.gadens.com/legal-insights/a-system-under-strain-the-unsustainable-rise-in-fair-work-commission-applications/)
    A self-declared problem creates a clear opening for case-management redesign, demand forecasting, digital triage and operating-model/efficiency work.
  • Hatcher described a 'perfect storm': ~75% workload growth versus three years ago, a requirement to cut ~11% from the budget, an actual ~$2m in-year cut (funding ~$111m in 2024/25) with no return to prior levels until 2028-29, and AI inflating application volumes (>40% of applicants admit using AI). (QLS Proctor, May 2026 — https://www.qlsproctor.com.au/2026/05/fair-work-commission-president-reveals-perfect-storm-as-ai-funding-cuts-hit/)
    Strong demand for cost-reduction strategy, workforce planning and AI-in-tribunal process controls — but note the entity is in budget-repair mode, constraining discretionary spend.
  • Employer groups and unions have repeatedly criticised member appointments as politically driven (AREEA's Steve Knott said the approach 'devalues the commission'); appointments are a long-running partisan flashpoint over the tribunal's perceived impartiality. (Lawyers Weekly, 26 Feb 2025 — https://www.lawyersweekly.com.au/politics/41588-labor-accused-of-stacking-fwc-with-union-and-lawyer-mates)
    Any engagement must be politically neutral and defensible to both employer and union stakeholders; manage bipartisan sensitivity.
  • The Commission established a 'Paid Agents Working Group' and published a report flagging that paid agents — not bound by lawyers' professional/ethical obligations — are a growing conduct concern in proceedings. (Gadens, 3 Dec 2025 — https://www.gadens.com/legal-insights/a-system-under-strain-the-unsustainable-rise-in-fair-work-commission-applications/)
    Scope for integrity-framework, regulatory-design, conduct-monitoring and stakeholder-engagement advisory.

Parliamentary matters

(ParlInfo (aph.gov.au), Estimates Hansard 31/05/2023 — https://parlinfo.aph.gov.au/parlInfo/search/display/display.w3p;query=Id:%22committees%2Festimate%2F26913%2F0002%22)
    Implementation of bargaining reforms is under active parliamentary scrutiny; any reform-support engagement would have visible parliamentary exposure and estimates-grade transparency expectations.
  • The Senate Education and Employment Legislation Committee held a March 2025 Estimates session featuring the Fair Work Commission, confirming a continuing scrutiny cadence. (Australian Parliament (APH) Senate Estimates video, 7 Mar 2025 — https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VoWdi66CyfE)
    Useful for timing engagement and anticipating questions on caseload, funding and reform delivery.
