Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research Organisation (CSIRO)
Industry, Science and Resources · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

CSIRO is Australia's national science agency (a corporate Commonwealth entity with significant own-source revenue) operating across six research areas plus national research infrastructure such as RV Investigator. The published 2025-26 plan landing page is extremely thin on detail, but its explicit framing around 'simplification and sustainability' confirms the dominant story external sources tell: CSIRO is mid-way through a financially-driven restructure targeting ~$100m in savings, with 800+ roles already cut (including ~25% of enterprise/support services) and further reductions flagged. This creates clear, defensible demand for operating-model and cost transformation, financial-sustainability advice, grants/compliance assurance (an ANAO focus), and workforce transition support. The constraint is equally clear: every external dollar must demonstrate hard value-for-money and withstand union, cross-bench and Senate Estimates scrutiny, and must not duplicate in-house capability.

Strategic priorities

  • Simplification and sustainability — CSIRO is explicitly reorienting toward increasing national impact 'through simplification and sustainability' — code for streamlining enterprise/support functions and putting the cost base on a sustainable footing while protecting research output.
  • Delivering impact across six research areas — The strategy directs focus and investment across six research areas plus the national collaborative research infrastructure it manages on behalf of the nation, executed through strategic priorities and key initiatives that drive performance.
  • Partnering across the innovation ecosystem — CSIRO positions itself as a connector working with government, research organisations, industry and community to deliver impact — with co-investment partnerships (e.g. Ningaloo Outlook with Woodside) central to its funding model.

Reform & change drivers

  • Financially-driven enterprise restructure targeting at least $100m in savings, including ~25% reduction in Enterprise Services (IT, finance, admin, comms, general management).
  • Operating costs (ICT and property) rising faster than inflation, and the expiry of a time-limited COVID-era budget measure, squeezing the cost base.
  • Decline in private-sector co-investment revenue (from $88.4m to $76.2m) intensifying value-for-money pressure on all spend.
  • Alignment expectations with the National Science and Research Priorities, framing decisions on research capability (notably climate/environment science).
  • Retrenchment of international/commercialisation footprint (e.g. closure of the US Delaware subsidiary).

Challenges & risks

  • Sustaining research output while cutting support services by a quarter — risk of capability and continuity loss.
  • Politically and reputationally sensitive cuts to environment/climate-modelling capability under cross-bench and Senate scrutiny.
  • Rising employee and redundancy costs (redundancy spend ~$11m in 2023-24; employee costs up >$100m to $984m) against a thinning revenue base.
  • Grants administration and Commonwealth Grant Rules and Guidelines compliance under ANAO attention.
  • Historic property/asset-strategy weaknesses re-emerging under current cost pressure.
  • Heightened union sensitivity and intense external scrutiny of any new external consulting spend.

In the news & controversies

  • CEO Dr Doug Hilton announced in 2024 that 375–500 Enterprise Services roles (IT, admin, finance, comms, general management) would be cut to save at least $100m; the Staff Association called it a 'dark day' and warned it could 'cripple research output.' (ACS Information Age — https://ia.acs.org.au/article/2024/up-to-500-jobs-to-be-cut-at-csiro.html ; CSIRO Staff Association compilation — https://csirostaff.org.au/news/media/2024/07/01/recent-media-on-csiro-job-cuts/)
    Large support-services restructure signals demand for operating-model and shared-services redesign, but also intense scrutiny and budget constraint on any external spend.
  • More than 800 jobs were cut after the first round in April 2024 with ~350 more flagged; the environment research unit lost 92 roles and was reduced from eight programs to five. Senator David Pocock criticised the reduction in 'sovereign climate modelling capacity.' (Canberra Times — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9251131/csiro-confirms-92-environment-research-jobs-cut-after-review/ ; Mirage News — https://www.miragenews.com/environmental-research-hit-by-widespread-csiro-1678727/)
    Research-prioritisation and capability-exit decisions are politically charged — advisory work here must be defensible and aligned to national science priorities.
  • CSIRO's 2023-24 accounts showed redundancy spend of ~$11m (up ~$4m), operating result down $3m to $14.7m, employee costs up >$100m to $984m, and private-sector revenue down from $88.4m to $76.2m (IP revenue $61m, a nine-year high). (Research Professional News — https://www.researchprofessionalnews.com/rr-news-australia-government-agencies-2024-11-redundancy-costs-rise-at-australian-science-agency/)
    Falling co-investment revenue and a rising cost base reinforce a hard value-for-money lens — pitches must show ROI and avoid duplicating in-house capability.
  • CSIRO's only US subsidiary, registered in Delaware as a commercialisation entity, was slated for closure in 2024 as part of the enterprise restructure. (InnovationAus via CSIRO Staff Association — https://csirostaff.org.au/news/media/2024/07/01/recent-media-on-csiro-job-cuts/)
    Indicates retrenchment of the international commercialisation footprint — opportunity lies in restructuring commercialisation models rather than expanding them.

ANAO audit findings

  • ANAO listed a potential performance audit on whether CSIRO's award and administration of funding is effective and consistent with the Commonwealth Grant Rules and Guidelines, examining programs including CSIRO Kick-Start, the SIEF Ross Metcalf Fellowship and SME Connect. Final tabling status was not confirmed. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/effectiveness-csiro-grants-allocation-and-administration ; SmartyGrants — https://www.smartygrants.com.au/articles/grants-watch-audits-investigations-and-reviews)
    Grants compliance and CGRG alignment are a live ANAO focus at CSIRO — a credible assurance/advisory entry point if the audit proceeds or recommendations follow.
  • An earlier (2020) ANAO performance audit found the design and implementation of CSIRO's 2012 Property Investment Strategy 'largely not effective,' with sites rising from 55 to 58 (2012–2019) even as building footprint fell ~10%. This predates the 3-year window and is background only. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/implementation-the-csiro-property-investment-strategy)
    Property/asset strategy is a documented historical weakness, newly relevant given current ICT/property cost pressure.

Parliamentary matters

  • At a Senate Economics Committee hearing on 7 November 2024, Dr Hilton testified the cuts were forced by ICT and property costs rising faster than inflation and the end of a COVID budget measure, requiring ~25% savings in enterprise services. On 25 November CSIRO confirmed RV Investigator would have full sea operations and no planned cuts to Data61. (Research Professional News (reporting Senate hearings) — https://www.researchprofessionalnews.com/rr-news-australia-government-agencies-2024-11-redundancy-costs-rise-at-australian-science-agency/)
    Estimates is actively scrutinising the restructure, vessel funding and Data61 — engagements must be defensible under parliamentary scrutiny, and Data61/digital is a comparatively protected unit.
  • Independent Senator David Pocock publicly criticised the latest cuts, particularly to climate-modelling capacity, arguing they conflict with the National Science and Research Priorities. (Canberra Times — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9251131/csiro-confirms-92-environment-research-jobs-cut-after-review/)
    Cross-bench attention raises reputational sensitivity around capability decisions; advisory positioning should emphasise alignment with stated national science priorities.
