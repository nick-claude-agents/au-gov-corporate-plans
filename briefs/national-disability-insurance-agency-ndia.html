National Disability Insurance Agency (NDIA)
Social Services (note: external reporting suggests NDIS responsibility may have moved to the new Department of Health, Disability and Ageing — confirm current portfolio before engaging) · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (4-year outlook) · BD confidence: Low

The NDIA is a large service-delivery agency under intense political and fiscal pressure to make the NDIS financially sustainable while protecting participants. Its 2025-26 Corporate Plan is built around implementing the 'Getting the NDIS Back on Track No.1' Act 2024 (effective 3 October 2024), a new legislative function and key activity targeting fraud and the misuse/abuse of NDIS funds, and the Participant Service Charter/Guarantee with new decision timeframes. New performance measures and revised targets have been introduced under Program 1.2. External signals reinforce a dominant cost-sustainability narrative (an 8% growth target, eligibility reassessments, provider pricing tensions and rising disputes), creating clear, time-bound demand for reform delivery, integrity assurance, financial/actuarial work and participant-facing service redesign. The full PDF was not available for this analysis — opportunities below are grounded in the plan's stated changes plus external research and should be validated against the full document.

Strategic priorities

  • Implementing the 'Getting the NDIS Back on Track' reforms — Operationalising the wide-ranging legislative changes from the NDIS Amendment Act 2024 that took effect 3 October 2024, which reshape access, planning, budgeting and scheme rules.
  • Fraud and misuse of funds control — A new legislative function and a new key activity (under Program 1.2) addressing risks related to fraud and the misuse and abuse of NDIS funds.
  • Participant Service Guarantee and service improvement — Delivering against the Participant Service Charter's five engagement principles and new statutory-style timeframes (the Participant Service Guarantee) for access, plan approvals, implementation, plan reviews and nominee changes, plus the practical changes in the Participant Service Improvement Plan.
  • Refreshed performance framework — New performance measures and updated performance targets accompanying the reform changes, signalling an active reshaping of how the Agency evidences scheme performance.

Reform & change drivers

  • NDIS Amendment (Getting the NDIS Back on Track No.1) Act 2024 — major legislative reform effective 3 October 2024
  • New legislative function on fraud and misuse/abuse of NDIS funds
  • Participant Service Guarantee introducing binding decision timeframes
  • National Cabinet 8% scheme-growth moderation target and associated eligibility reassessments
  • Cost-sustainability pressure (spending materially above earlier forecasts)
  • Provider market instability and recurring Annual Pricing Review tensions
  • Possible machinery-of-government change moving the NDIS into a new health/disability/ageing department

Challenges & risks

  • Fraud, misuse and abuse of NDIS funds — now an explicit legislative function and enterprise concern
  • Balancing cost moderation (8% target, eligibility reassessments) against participant harm and reputational risk
  • Rising volume of disputed plan decisions and growing external legal costs
  • Provider sustainability and pricing adequacy threatening market viability
  • Meeting new Participant Service Guarantee timeframes at scale across high transaction volumes
  • Delivering complex legislative reform while maintaining BAU service delivery

In the news & controversies

  • An AFR opinion piece (26 Sept 2024) framed NDIS cost growth as adding to inflation and rate pressure, drawing pushback from advocates who called the 'economic burden' framing misleading while conceding fraud, red tape and accountability need urgent reform. (Physical Disability Australia response to AFR — https://www.pda.org.au/2024/09/27/physical-disability-australias-response-to-john-kehoes-afr-article-ndis-cost-blowout-adds-to-inflation-and-rates-pressure-dated-september-26th-september-2024/)
    Cost-sustainability is the dominant political narrative driving efficiency and integrity demand, but advisers must navigate a sensitive 'savings vs participant harm' debate.
  • Reporting indicates NDIS spending well above earlier estimates, alongside a sharp rise in participants disputing plan decisions at the tribunal and growing NDIA spend on external legal firms. (The Conversation — https://theconversation.com/what-we-know-about-the-ndis-cuts-and-what-theyll-mean-for-people-with-disability-and-their-families-179748 (note: some figures may be dated/superseded))
    Pipeline in actuarial/forecasting, decision-quality and internal-review/dispute-reduction work the Agency is under pressure to fix.
  • National Cabinet set an 8% annual growth target (scheme still projected near $58bn by 2028); the NDIA has been reassessing participant eligibility, with reports of 28-day evidence deadlines affecting participants including children. (Hellocare (May 2025) — https://hellocare.com.au/ndis-budget-blowout-sparks-eligibility-crackdown/)
    Live demand for eligibility-process design, reform implementation and participant communications/assurance; high reputational sensitivity.
  • National Disability Services warned that inadequate NDIA pricing and rising delivery costs pushed quality providers out of the market, framing the next Annual Pricing Review as a possible 'last chance' to prevent market failure (May 2024). (ParlInfo (NDS media release, 14/05/2024) — https://parlinfo.aph.gov.au/parlInfo/search/)
    Market-design, pricing-review and provider-sustainability advisory needs are pressing and recurrent.
  • The Centre for Independent Studies argued early design flaws and cost overruns flagged by successive actuarial (ASFR) reports were not corrected, and that public reporting on the NDIA's response was lacking. (Centre for Independent Studies — https://www.cis.org.au/publication/a-better-track-for-ndis-reform-fix-the-flaws-that-caused-blowout/)
    Governance, transparency and assurance-framework work positions a firm around accountability rather than pure cost-cutting.

Parliamentary matters
