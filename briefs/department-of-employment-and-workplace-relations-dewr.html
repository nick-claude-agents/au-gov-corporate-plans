Department of Employment and Workplace Relations (DEWR)
Employment and Workplace Relations · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

After three years leading major reforms across employment, skills and workplace relations, DEWR's stated focus for 2025-26 is delivery — making existing initiatives produce measurable outcomes. The department is a hybrid policy/service-delivery entity (~4,454 APS staff) running employment services to ~700,000 jobseekers, VET and apprenticeship programs, workplace-relations policy, and a newly assumed role administering the CFMEU's Construction and General Division. Strong BD signals sit in: embedding employment services reform and fixing the mutual-obligations compliance system (acutely Robodebt-sensitive), an Integrity Roadmap (2025-27), a governance-framework review, expanding AI/data deployment, ICT modernisation and Essential Eight cyber uplift, and maturing a performance/regulatory framework. The major constraint is the Strategic Commissioning Framework: DEWR is deliberately reducing outsourcing of core work (policy, legal, finance, service delivery), so credible opportunities skew toward independent assurance, ICT/digital build, evaluation and specialist surge rather than core advisory.

Strategic priorities

  • Shift from reform design to delivery — DEWR explicitly pivots from three years of reform to ensuring those reforms deliver effective outcomes — implementing election commitments and embedding employment services reforms (parents, paid work placements, justice-to-employment transitions).
  • Fix the mutual obligations compliance system — Ensuring the mutual obligations compliance system 'operates as intended' with a fair, transparent framework — a politically charged area given the Robodebt legacy.
  • Joined-up tertiary system and VET reform — Working with the new Australian Tertiary Education Commission, JSA and Department of Education on a tertiary system roadmap aligning VET and higher education to labour-market needs; delivering Free TAFE and apprenticeship incentive reform.
  • AI adoption — internal and national policy — Expanding internal AI deployment to lift productivity and service delivery, while contributing to national AI workforce policy via the tripartite AI Working Group.
  • Construction industry / CFMEU administration — Driving structural and cultural reform in construction through the National Construction Industry Forum and administering the CFMEU Construction and General Division and its branches.
  • Integrity maturity uplift — Implementing the Integrity Roadmap (2025-2027) developed with an independent advisor, including Robodebt Royal Commission recommendations and conflict-of-interest/PID capabilities.
  • New and evolving regulatory functions — Progressing a national labour hire licensing scheme, maturing the department's regulatory performance framework, and reviewing multiple legislative frameworks (Secure Jobs Better Pay, Closing Loopholes, SRC Act, JSA enabling legislation).

Reform & change drivers

  • Government economic-growth agenda focused on lifting productivity, building resilience and budget sustainability
  • Persistent structural labour-market challenges — one-third of occupations in national shortage, 61.4% of employment-services participants in the system over 12 months
  • Net zero transition requiring workforce reskilling and place-based community support
  • Multiple legislative reviews: Secure Jobs Better Pay Act, Closing Loopholes Act, SRC Act 1988 (Comcare), and JSA enabling legislation
  • Robodebt Royal Commission response and broader public-sector integrity reform agenda
  • APS Reform agenda and the Strategic Commissioning Framework reducing outsourcing of core work
  • National Skills Agreement shared-stewardship model and creation of the Australian Tertiary Education Commission
  • Closing the Gap — new First Nations Executive Board governance and 2025 Implementation Plan commitments
  • Accelerating AI adoption creating both internal productivity demand and national policy/regulatory pressure

Challenges & risks

  • Delivery risk: ability to deliver programs on time/budget amid reforms, competing priorities and 'reduced funding' / resourcing constraints
  • Mutual obligations compliance system must be demonstrably fair and transparent — high public and political scrutiny post-Robodebt
  • People risk: competitive labour market, difficulty attracting/retaining skilled staff, and staff burnout / psychosocial safety
  • ICT risk: inability to meet demand for skilled ICT labour, ageing/historical systems, cyber and concentration threats, funding pressure on ICT program
  • Information and data risk: breaches, unauthorised access, data bias including from AI use
  • Finance and regulatory risk: budget constraints, fraud/corruption/privacy exposure, and legislative compliance capability gaps
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework limits use of external providers for core policy, legal, finance and service-delivery work
  • Stakeholder fatigue across an unusually broad partnership network (unions, employers, states, First Nations, international)
