Attorney-General's Department
Attorney-General · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

The Attorney-General's Department (AGD) is a policy and legal-services department (with a self-funded legal arm in the Australian Government Solicitor) responsible for Australia's law, justice, security and integrity frameworks. For 2025-26 it is leading the Commonwealth's integrity agenda (an APS Integrity Strategy, fraud control, Public Interest Disclosure, privacy reforms), driving First Nations justice and Closing the Gap reforms, responding to the family and domestic violence crisis through the National Access to Justice Partnership 2025-30, and grappling with the policy and operational implications of AI. The department openly signals capability pressure on ICT sustainability, cyber uplift, data maturity, risk-function maturity and workforce conversion under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework. As a policy/regulatory department it offers strongest BD fit in Strategy, Policy & Governance; Audit & Assurance; Data, Analytics & Information; Digital & ICT Delivery; and Programs, Projects & Change — sized proportionately, since the Strategic Commissioning Framework is actively reducing outsourcing of core ICT work.

Strategic priorities

  • Lead the Commonwealth integrity agenda — Develop an Integrity Strategy for the Commonwealth Public Service, steward the Commonwealth Fraud and Corruption Control Framework, Public Interest Disclosure legislation and government transparency measures, and build counter-fraud capability across government via the Commonwealth Fraud Prevention Centre.
  • Privacy reform — Advise on and progress reforms to the Privacy Act and coordinate government policy on data access, protection and sharing.
  • First Nations justice and Closing the Gap — Progress First Nations Justice policy and work under the National Agreement on Closing the Gap, embedding the Four Priority Reforms, including Justice Reinvestment and trauma-informed services.
  • Family and domestic violence and access to justice — Respond to the FDV national crisis and deliver the National Access to Justice Partnership 2025-30 with states and territories, funding Legal Aid Commissions, ATSILS and Community Legal Centres.
  • Responsible adoption of AI — Embrace AI to streamline processes and enhance decision-making and policy development, while advising government on AI regulation, transparency and safeguarding against misuse.
  • Criminal law fit-for-purpose and Pacific partnerships — Ensure criminal law and frameworks remain fit for purpose and build law-and-justice partnerships in the Pacific and Indo-Pacific.

Reform & change drivers

  • Government integrity agenda — new APS Integrity Strategy, fraud control framework stewardship, PID legislation, NACC environment
  • Privacy Act reforms and whole-of-government data access/protection/sharing policy
  • National Access to Justice Partnership 2025-30 — new five-year intergovernmental funding agreement
  • National Agreement on Closing the Gap and the Four Priority Reforms
  • Rise of AI driving both policy/regulatory demand and internal process change
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework — mandated reduction of outsourcing of core work, especially ICT & Digital Solutions ($2.19m reduction target)
  • New regulatory performance reporting obligations under the Regulator Performance RMG
  • ICT sustainability and rising technology costs; cloud adoption and cyber uplift agenda
  • Heightened national security threat environment and online extremist content affecting social cohesion

Challenges & risks

  • Strategic risk that policy solutions are ineffective or legal/policy advice is flawed
  • Risk of failing to protect people, information and assets in Australia and overseas (security/cyber)
  • Risk of failing to comply with law and integrity frameworks
  • Explicit risk of failing to maintain financial sustainability
  • Workforce risk — attracting, recruiting and retaining people with the right skills, including digital, data and HR capability
  • ICT environment sustainability pressure given increasing technology costs
  • Risk function still maturing — developing a control effectiveness framework and emerging-risk processes
  • Demand pressure on courts and legal assistance from FDV crisis and cost-of-living-driven family law disputes
  • Tension between reducing outsourcing (Strategic Commissioning) and capability gaps, constraining external spend on core ICT
