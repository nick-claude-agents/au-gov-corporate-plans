Australian Federal Police (AFP)
Home Affairs · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

The AFP's republished 2025-26 Corporate Plan signals a deliberate repositioning as a national security agency under new Commissioner Krissy Barrett, with five enterprise priorities (defending domestic/regional security, protecting vulnerable communities, supercharging global operations, futureproofing the workforce, and investing in science and technology-led capability). The plan openly admits investment pressure: legacy ICT requiring modernisation, an ambitious AI and big-data agenda needing responsible-use governance, a competitive recruitment market, and budget/cost-of-doing-business pressure within a fixed envelope. A completed capability framework review (18 core capabilities) with roadmaps now being developed is the single clearest source of structured, multi-year demand. Major named programs — the AUKUS nuclear-submarine protective security overlay, the Pacific Police Partnership Program, and a refreshed performance framework — create time-bound delivery, assurance and evaluation opportunities. As a security/enforcement archetype, engagement will be constrained by clearances and secrecy, so enabling/corporate functions (capability governance, workforce, data, ICT modernisation, performance) are the most accessible entry points.

Strategic priorities

  • Investing in science and technology-led capability — The AFP is prioritising AI and big-data tooling overlaid with responsible/ethical-use governance, enhancing data capabilities, modernising legacy ICT to defensible architecture, and improving continuous threat exposure management.
  • Futureproofing the workforce — The AFP wants a thriving, diverse, skills-based workforce and is implementing the Health and Wellbeing Strategy, reviewing training and leadership curriculums, streamlining recruitment gateways, and shifting complaints management from a response model to a prevention culture.
  • Supercharging global operations — Amplifying AFP global impact through forward-looking law-enforcement partnerships, AUKUS uplift, the Pacific Police Partnership Program, and hosting the UN Chiefs of Police Summit in 2026.
  • Capability framework roadmaps — Having completed a systematic review consolidating to 18 core capabilities, the AFP's key body of work for the year is developing updated capability roadmaps with governance for investment prioritisation, reduced duplication and sustainability.
  • AUKUS protective security overlay — Designing and delivering a fit-for-purpose interim protective security overlay supporting nuclear-powered submarine visits at HMAS Stirling ahead of Submarine Rotational Force West.
  • Performance framework uplift — The AFP is reforming its performance measures — introducing 'partially met' thresholds, removing low-value ROI measures, adding child-exploitation and refining Pacific measures — with further improvements planned for 2026-27 and beyond.

Reform & change drivers

  • New Commissioner (October 2025) and a new mission redefining the AFP as a national security agency, supported by a new Ministerial Direction and Statement of Intent
  • AUKUS initiative driving a new protective security role around nuclear-powered submarines
  • Australian Government policy for responsible use of AI requiring a transparency statement and technology-governance uplift
  • 2023-2030 Australian Cyber Security Strategy driving legacy ICT modernisation and defensible architecture
  • Pacific and South-East Asia transnational crime threat driving the Pacific Police Partnership Program and 2026 UN Chiefs of Police Summit hosting
  • National Strategy to Prevent and Respond to Child Sexual Abuse creating new performance commitments
  • Completed capability framework review (18 core capabilities) requiring roadmaps and investment prioritisation
  • Competitive labour market and recruitment challenges for skills-based and diverse talent

Challenges & risks

  • Budget and cost-of-doing-business pressure within a fixed budgetary envelope, requiring organisational efficiencies (Resourcing enterprise risk)
  • Legacy ICT requiring transition to modern, defensible systems and platforms
  • Workforce risk: failure to recruit, retain and develop staff with the right skills in a competitive market, plus ageing workforce
  • Information risk: failure to access, manage, store or protect AFP information including third-party-held data, with privacy and reputational implications
  • Effectiveness of capabilities risk: ineffective management/bundling of resources restricting innovation
  • Culture, standards and integrity risk, with oversight from NACC, Commonwealth Ombudsman and IGIS
  • Responsible/ethical AI adoption must align with legislative frameworks and government policy
  • Health, safety and wellbeing risk to the workforce; psychological and cultural safety in complaints management
