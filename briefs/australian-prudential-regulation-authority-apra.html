Australian Prudential Regulation Authority (APRA)
Treasury · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-26 to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Medium

APRA is a mid-sized, expertise-heavy financial regulator (archetype: regulator) levy-funded with an approved average staffing level of 907. The 2025-26 plan pivots from a decade of post-Royal Commission framework build-up toward maintaining resilience, 'getting the balance right' on competition/efficiency (nine burden-reduction initiatives responding to a July 2025 Treasurer/Finance Minister letter), and lifting its own organisational effectiveness. The richest BD signals sit in its data/technology modernisation program — a $73.2m investment across five initiatives (a new cloud data platform by Dec 2025, full transition to APRA Connect by Dec 2027, an upgraded supervision management system going live Nov 2025), plus AI adoption, cyber/privacy uplift and a supervisory development centre. New performance measures, a refreshed governance standard, the IMF FSAP review, a system stress test and multiple ANAO/parliamentary touchpoints create assurance, evaluation, governance and workforce demand. Because APRA largely builds internally and is clearance/secrecy-sensitive on supervisory data, offerings should be proportionate and weighted toward advisory, assurance, data and delivery-support rather than large transactional programs.

Strategic priorities

  • Maintaining financial and operational resilience — Maintain (rather than expand) the prudential framework through targeted standard changes — finalise bank capital (Additional Tier 1 removal, effective Jan 2027), commence liquidity-framework revisions, refresh governance standards, and embed CPS 230 Operational Resilience (effective 1 July 2025) and CPS 190/CPS 900 recovery, exit and resolution planning.
  • Responding to significant and emerging risks — Address geopolitical risk, cyber-attacks, third-party/interconnection risk, AI adoption, climate and housing-lending vulnerabilities — including an inaugural system stress test, a geopolitical risk workplan, a material service provider register (due 1 Oct 2025) and a general-insurance Climate Vulnerability Assessment.
  • Getting the balance right (competition, efficiency and burden reduction) — Progress nine actions to reduce regulatory burden and support productivity in response to the Treasurer/Finance Minister's July 2025 letter, including a new third proportionality tier, simplifying bank licensing (halving processing time), payments/SVF reform readiness, and new public performance measures emphasising competition and efficiency.
  • Improving organisational effectiveness (data, technology, people) — Deliver a $73.2m data-capability program: a secure cloud data platform (Dec 2025), full APRA Connect transition and legacy retirement (Dec 2027), an upgraded supervision management system (Nov 2025), AI adoption, cyber/privacy uplift, and a new supervisory development centre and leadership/remuneration reforms.

Reform & change drivers

  • July 2025 letter from the Treasurer and Minister for Finance seeking specific, measurable actions to reduce compliance costs — driving the nine burden-reduction initiatives.
  • $73.2m FY2024-25 Budget funding for five data-capability initiatives (cloud platform, APRA Connect transition, data governance, cyber/privacy controls, supervision management system upgrade).
  • Payments regulation reform expanding APRA's role over Stored Value Facilities and payment stablecoins, requiring new prudential standards once legislation is set.
  • CPS 230 Operational Resilience (effective 1 July 2025), CPS 190 Recovery & Exit Planning and CPS 900 Resolution Planning embedding new obligations.
  • Government Regulatory Initiatives Grid and CFR coordination shaping timing and cross-regulator alignment.
  • Independent IMF Financial Sector Assessment Program review of APRA's framework later in the year.
  • New PGPA-aligned, outcomes-focused performance measures with a stronger competition/efficiency emphasis.
  • Ageing population reshaping superannuation (retirement phase) and health/life insurance demand, plus a new retirement reporting framework (commencing 2027 with Treasury).

Challenges & risks

  • Stated key risk: insufficient capacity/capability and organisational mechanisms to manage competing priorities and reprioritise/redeploy resources.
  • Inadequate operational resilience to secure people, data and third-party services in a heightened cyber and threat environment.
  • Integrity and regulatory-capture risk to staff conduct, values and reputation (APRA is under National Anti-Corruption Commission jurisdiction).
  • Governance weaknesses across regulated entities — almost 80% of entities under heightened supervision exhibit underlying governance issues — driving a governance-standard refresh.
  • Housing-lending vulnerabilities as interest rates decline (higher leverage, riskier lending), under CFR monitoring.
  • Insurance affordability/availability pressure from climate events; 4% of households uninsured and 7% underinsured.
  • Workforce capability strain as supervision becomes more complex; need for sustained training, leadership and remuneration reform within a fixed ASL of 907.
  • Legacy data systems requiring substantial manual effort, being retired by Dec 2027.
