This brief therefore reads TEQSA's likely demand signals from the department's reform agenda that directly reshapes TEQSA's operating context, rather than from TEQSA's own stated priorities. The standout signals affecting TEQSA are: the establishment of the Australian Tertiary Education Commission (ATEC) as higher-education steward from 1 July 2025 (interim) and 1 January 2026 (permanent); a new standalone regulatory framework on provider responses to gender-based violence; a new framework to lift the quality and integrity of international education; and counter-foreign-interference work in the university sector. TEQSA is a classic expertise-heavy regulator: demand will centre on regulatory-framework design, data/intelligence capability, and assurance of regulatory effectiveness — not large delivery programs. Strategic priorities

  • Establishment of ATEC and reshaping the higher education regulatory architecture — The government is standing up the Australian Tertiary Education Commission as steward of higher education, interim from 1 July 2025 and permanently (subject to legislation) from 1 January 2026. This redraws the institutional landscape around TEQSA and creates questions about role boundaries, data sharing and operating model alignment between TEQSA, ATEC and the department.
  • New standalone regulatory framework on gender-based violence in higher education — The department will establish a new standalone regulatory framework requiring higher education providers to meet best-practice standards to prevent and respond to gender-based violence. As the higher-education regulator, TEQSA is the likely administrator of new provider obligations, requiring new regulatory processes, guidance and monitoring.
  • Integrity and sustainability of international education — A new framework will improve the quality and integrity of international education, with continued work to strengthen and make the sector more sustainable. This places fresh regulatory and intelligence demands on TEQSA in relation to provider integrity and student outcomes.
  • Countering foreign interference in the university sector — The department will work with the sector to implement the Guidelines to counter foreign interference in the Australian university sector — an area where TEQSA's regulatory and assurance posture intersects with security expectations.
  • Closer integration of higher education and VET — The government intends to bring higher education and vocational training closer together, which over the plan period will affect qualification recognition, provider regulation and TEQSA's regulatory perimeter.

Reform & change drivers

  • Australian Universities Accord implementation reshaping higher education funding, equity and quality settings
  • Establishment of ATEC as a new steward body, altering institutional roles around TEQSA
  • New standalone gender-based violence regulatory framework for higher education providers (subject to legislation)
  • New international education quality and integrity framework, plus sustainability pressures on the sector
  • Guidelines to counter foreign interference in the university sector
  • Tertiary harmonisation — bringing higher education and VET closer together
  • Regulator Performance principles (RMG 128) and a published Statement of Expectations / Regulatory Statement of Intent governing how regulation is delivered
  • Rapid evolution of AI affecting academic integrity and the regulated population

  • Data and intelligence demands of risk-based regulation across a large, diverse provider population
  • Maintaining regulatory effectiveness and public trust under RMG 128 best-practice principles
  • International education sustainability pressures increasing provider-integrity caseload
