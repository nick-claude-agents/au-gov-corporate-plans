Defence Housing Australia
Defence · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

Defence Housing Australia (DHA) is a corporate Commonwealth entity and Government Business Enterprise that provides housing and housing-related services to ~61,189 permanent ADF members, a population forecast to grow to 80,000 by 2040. The 2025-26 Statement of Corporate Intent sets a clear, investment-heavy agenda built on four objectives: a modern, customer-centric operating model enabled by rapid digitisation; building an agile, scalable housing capability to support ADF growth; embedding ESG (including new Commonwealth climate disclosure obligations); and fiscal responsibility / long-term financial stability. The strongest BD signals are the end-to-end customer-interface digitisation program, the strategic provisioning / portfolio-growth ('case for growth') agenda, a new Asset Management program targeting repairs-and-maintenance cost reduction, and an explicit ESG baseline-and-strategy build. As a self-funded GBE under cost and capital-access pressure, DHA is a credible prospect for digital delivery, financial-sustainability/costing, program delivery and governance/assurance work — sized to a focused, commercially-disciplined organisation.

Strategic priorities

  • Digitise the customer and property operating model — DHA is committed to moving 'faster to deliver end to end digital services' across tenancy, postings, enquiry management, rent allowance, property management, online maintenance and inspections, with a multi-year roadmap of customer-interface digitisation projects from 2025-26 through 2028-29.
  • The case for growth — scalable housing capability — A deliberate shift towards DHA playing a larger role in the ADF's housing requirements, growing the owned/leased portfolio to meet ADF growth to 80,000 by 2040, developing new sourcing channels and product models, and embedding them into BAU.
  • ESG baseline and strategy — DHA will establish an ESG baseline defining how existing programs contribute to ESG objectives, then write an ESG Strategy setting future activities and targets, while meeting EPBC Act compliance and new Commonwealth Climate Disclosure obligations.
  • Fiscal responsibility and asset-management cost reduction — DHA is pursuing strong value for money and long-term financial stability, including an Asset Management program to reduce repairs and maintenance costs, digitise asset accounting and condition assessments, and introduce strategic procurement, with a 3% return-on-equity target.
  • Formalise future housing needs with Defence — DHA will agree and formalise future housing needs through Future Services Agreement and Housing Policy projects to ensure certainty in housing supply against the Approved Provisioning Schedule.

Reform & change drivers

  • ADF workforce growth from ~61,189 to 80,000 members by 2040, aligned to the National Defence Strategy 2024, Defence Strategic Review 2023 and Defence Workforce Plan 2024-2034 — driving portfolio growth and provisioning demand.
  • Constrained housing markets with tight rental supply, low construction completions and falling greenfield land release, especially in priority regions (WA and northern Australia) flagged in the National Defence Strategy.
  • Access to capital pressure: DHA is self-funded through rents, fees, debt financing and land sales and must achieve a sustainable portfolio of owned and leased properties.
  • New mandatory Commonwealth Climate Disclosure obligations (developed by Finance) starting 2024-25 and EPBC Act compliance requirements.
  • Advances in technology including agentic AI and 'industrial agent' customer-service solutions reshaping the digital customer experience.
  • A planned holistic review of KPIs in 2025-26 to be reflected in the 2026-27 PBS and SCI.
  • Increased climate/disaster risk exposure for the portfolio as more ADF members are based in Northern Australia.

Challenges & risks

  • Financial sustainability of a self-funded GBE that must fund portfolio growth while delivering a 3% return on equity — balancing service culture with commercial culture.
  • Securing leased properties in constrained, priority markets while keeping pace with ADF growth demand.
  • Repairs and maintenance cost pressure across a large owned/leased portfolio (~15,760 DHA owned/leased solutions).
  • Delivery and integration risk of a multi-year, end-to-end digitisation program touching tenancy, property management and finance systems.
  • Meeting new climate-disclosure and EPBC compliance obligations from a low ESG baseline ('it is early in DHA's trajectory').
  • Weak/newly-introduced performance measures — DHA itself is initiating a holistic KPI review, signalling the current framework is under question.
  • Strategic risk oversight: a KPI of zero strategic risks outside tolerance implies tight enterprise risk-control expectations.
