ANU is a corporate Commonwealth entity governed by its own federal legislation, making it unusually subject to ANAO audit and Senate Estimates for a university. It is in the middle of a contested transformation ('Renew ANU') targeting ~$250m in cost cuts and ~650 jobs, amid a disputed deficit narrative, leadership exits (VC and Chancellor both resigned in 2025), a Ministerial referral to regulator TEQSA, and recurring Senate scrutiny. There is genuine latent demand for financial-sustainability, governance and change-management support — but consultancy spend at ANU is itself a live political flashpoint, with contracts itemised through Questions on Notice and criticised in Estimates. Any BD pursuit must be probity-first and prepared for full public disclosure.

Strategic priorities

  • Cost reduction and financial sustainability ('Renew ANU') — Reduce operating costs by a stated ~$250m over ~15 months (≈$150m spending, ≈$100m salaries) and return to a sustainable financial footing following a contested 2024 deficit.
  • Organisational restructure — Collapse seven academic colleges into six (including disestablishing the College of Health and Medicine), with an accompanying operating-model and workforce change.
  • Governance and leadership reset under regulator oversight — Rebuild governance credibility and recruit a new Chancellor and Vice-Chancellor under TEQSA oversight following leadership resignations and a Ministerial referral.

Reform & change drivers

  • Stated structural operating deficit and a board/management decision to cut costs and restructure colleges
  • Ministerial referral to TEQSA over governance and conflict-of-interest concerns, placing the regulator inside ANU's governance and recruitment processes
  • Leadership transition — resignations of both the Vice-Chancellor (Sept 2025) and Chancellor (Oct 2025) and an interim VC reversing the forced-redundancy push
  • Sustained parliamentary scrutiny via Senate Estimates and a Senate inquiry into university governance
  • As a corporate Commonwealth entity, exposure to ANAO audit and Commonwealth-level accountability uncommon among universities

Challenges & risks

  • Publicly contested financial figures — external analysts allege the deficit was overstated by ~$60m and that audited accounts showed a surplus, undermining the cost-cutting rationale
  • Severe internal trust deficit: 750+ staff voted no confidence in the Chancellor and VC; strong NTEU opposition to external advisers perceived as management-aligned
  • Consultancy spend is politicised — the Nous contract (rising from $837k to $1.127m) drew QoN scrutiny and an allegation that ANU misled the Senate
  • Probity and conflict-of-interest exposure at the most senior levels, now under TEQSA examination
  • Decision-making continuity is uncertain during leadership transition, complicating engagement and approvals
