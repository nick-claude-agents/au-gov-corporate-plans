The ACCC (which also houses the Australian Energy Regulator) is an expertise-heavy economic regulator in the Treasury portfolio. The supplied corporate plan content is effectively a landing-page stub with no substantive body text, so this brief is built largely from external research and the ACCC's well-documented operating context rather than the plan itself. The dominant, time-bound demand signals are the new mandatory merger-notification regime commencing 1 January 2026, the funded supermarket/grocery enforcement program and price inquiry, the National Anti-Scam Centre, and repeated ANAO probity scrutiny. As a regulator, the best-fit opportunities lie in Strategy/Policy/Governance, Data & Analytics, Digital/ICT Delivery and Assurance/Probity rather than large transactional delivery programs.

Strategic priorities

  • Implementing the new merger control regime — From 1 January 2026 Australia moves to a mandatory, suspensory merger-notification system administered by the ACCC, requiring new processes, systems, case-handling capacity and economic analysis capability. This is the most significant structural change to the ACCC's functions in years.
  • Supermarket and retail competition focus — The ACCC received dedicated funding for supermarket/retail investigation and enforcement and was directed to run a 12-month grocery price inquiry, creating a funded, time-boxed program of market study and enforcement activity.
  • Scams disruption via the National Anti-Scam Centre — The ACCC hosts the NASC, requiring data-sharing, intelligence and analytics capability across banks, telcos and digital platforms.
  • Compliance and enforcement priorities — The ACCC sets annual compliance and enforcement priorities (e.g. misleading surcharging, consumer guarantees, digital platforms) and pursues major litigation such as ACCC v Qantas.

Reform & change drivers

  • Commencement of the mandatory merger-notification regime on 1 January 2026 — new legislative function requiring new processes and systems
  • $30m of dedicated supermarket/retail enforcement funding (over ~3.5 years) plus a government-directed 12-month grocery price inquiry
  • National Anti-Scam Centre operations requiring multi-party data sharing and analytics
  • High-profile, resource-intensive litigation (e.g. ACCC v Qantas 'ghost flights')
  • Repeated ANAO scrutiny of probity management within the ACCC

Challenges & risks

  • Standing up an entirely new merger-review function (people, process, systems) to a hard legislative deadline
  • Surge resourcing and analytical capacity to deliver both the grocery inquiry and ongoing enforcement
  • Integrity/probity exposure given the ACCC's market-sensitive information and the ANAO's repeated probity focus
  • Demonstrating regulatory effectiveness and outcomes through credible performance measures
  • Data integration and intelligence quality across the anti-scam ecosystem
