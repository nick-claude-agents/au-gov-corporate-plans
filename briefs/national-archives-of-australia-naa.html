National Archives of Australia (NAA)
Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications, Sport & Arts · Corporate Plan 2025-26 to 2028-29 · BD confidence: Medium

The National Archives is a medium-sized (~390 staff), non-corporate Commonwealth entity undertaking a strategy-driven transformation (Strategy 2025-2030: Evolving National Archives) centred on becoming a 'digital-first' archive. Its dominant pressures are the explosion of born-digital records, an ageing at-risk physical/AV collection requiring urgent digitisation, legacy manual workflows, cyber threat, and a tight fiscal position where the plan openly states expenses 'appear likely to exceed available appropriations'. The agency is also pursuing modernisation of the Archives Act 1983 and leads whole-of-government information management through the 'Building trust in the public record' policy. For BD purposes this is principally a Digital & ICT Delivery, Data/Information and Financial-Sustainability story, sized to a smaller cultural/data agency rather than large-scale program delivery. Note the plan is dated 2025-26 to 2028-29 despite the source URL referencing the 2024-25-2027-28 plan.

Strategic priorities

  • Build a digital-first, end-to-end archive — Transition all aspects of work to digital, accelerate development of a digital-first archive, streamline ingestion of born-digital records, integrate legacy systems and automate manual workflows.
  • Preserve and digitise at-risk collections — Prioritise digitisation and preservation of records at risk of loss or deterioration (chemically unstable, obsolete physical and digital formats), with a focus on First Nations material; meet targets of 20%+ of at-risk physical items digitised and 95%+ of digital items in low-risk stable formats.
  • Lead whole-of-government information management — Drive uptake of the 'Building trust in the public record' policy and lift information management maturity across agencies via the annual Check-up survey and targeted agency engagement; redevelop Agency Engagement and Digital Archiving Service models.
  • Modernise the Archives Act 1983 — Pursue amendments to the Archives Act, a Revive deliverable, to reflect the digital age and resolve ambiguities; the Act currently lacks any enforcement power over agency compliance.
  • Improve access, search and discovery — Deliver improvements in public search, external identity management, payment processing and content delivery; uplift metadata management and reparative/culturally appropriate description, including for First Nations collections.
  • Financial sustainability — Manage a structural gap where ongoing expenses are likely to exceed appropriations; pursue efficiencies, consolidate repositories, scale storage and review the state/territory service-delivery model.

Reform & change drivers

  • Strategy 2025-2030: Evolving National Archives, driving an organisation-wide transformation
  • 'Building trust in the public record' whole-of-government information management policy
  • National Cultural Policy (Revive) commitments, including 'First Nations first' and Archives Act reform
  • Archives Act 1983 modernisation agenda (no current enforcement power over agency compliance)
  • Shift to digital-by-default across government producing ever-growing volumes of born-digital records
  • Functional and Efficiency Review (Tune Review) and Government response implementation
  • Tight fiscal environment with expenses likely to exceed appropriations
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework targets to reduce outsourcing of Information and Knowledge Management work
  • Redevelopment of Agency Engagement and Digital Archiving Service models (reflected in revised PBS targets)

Challenges & risks

  • Digital archiving systems and practice not keeping pace with rapid technological change
  • Archival material lost or destroyed through obsolescence, resource limitations, neglect or hostile action
  • Agencies not meeting Archives Act / information management obligations (a shared risk, with no enforcement lever)
  • Delayed public access to the collection due to manual processes and limited resources
  • Failure to connect with audiences, particularly First Nations and diverse communities
  • Agency capability (people, processes, technology) not meeting operational requirements, especially digital-first workforce
  • Cyber security threat landscape evolving as threat-actor capability grows
  • Financial sustainability: structural gap between growing demand/costs and appropriations
  • Expanding storage needs (physical and digital) for a continually growing collection
