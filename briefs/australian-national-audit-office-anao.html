Australian National Audit Office (ANAO)
Parliament · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (four-year outlook to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Low

The ANAO is a small, specialist integrity agency that audits the Commonwealth on behalf of the Parliament. Its 2025-26 plan is dominated by enabling and corporate themes rather than program delivery: a flagged budget shortfall (performance audit targets cut from a JCPAA-recommended 48 to 38-42), a new digital strategy and AI policy, persistent workforce attrition and talent competition, and a drive to integrate and streamline its audit products. Because ANAO is itself the supreme audit institution and prizes independence, it is a cautious and conflict-sensitive buyer of external advice, and any work touching core audit functions is unlikely. Credible, proportionate opportunities sit in its corporate enablers — workforce/capability, digital and AI investment governance, and financial sustainability/costing — where independent advice does not compromise its mandate. Expect modest engagement sizes (short advisory or framework pieces), not multi-year delivery.

Strategic priorities

  • Impact where it matters — Maximise the value and use of audit work by the Parliament, support the establishment of the 48th Parliament and the JCPAA, deepen public sector engagement, and trial integrated financial/performance statements audits and a 'rapid review' performance audit approach to reduce duplication and improve timeliness.
  • Performance through innovation — Embed innovation in audit products and processes, adopt approved AI tools and an automation program to lift productivity and quality, apply a sharper risk focus to audit procedures, and develop a digital strategy to become a data-enabled organisation.
  • Inspire and develop our people — Build a skilled, adaptable, diverse workforce able to work flexibly across audit products; grow specialist capability (including climate-change disclosure auditing and AI use) via the ANAO Academy; and manage high attrition through workforce planning, development and retention.

Reform & change drivers

  • Budget constraint: current funding does not support the JCPAA-recommended 48 performance audits, with targets reduced to 38-42 reports to protect mandatory functions
  • Establishment of the 48th Parliament and re-establishment of the JCPAA, which reviews ANAO reports and budget
  • New audit product: ramping up performance statements audits and developing in-house capability for climate-change disclosure auditing
  • Adoption of AI and automation in audit work, requiring a new AI policy, safeguards and staff training
  • Development of a new ANAO digital strategy to become a 'data-enabled organisation'
  • Refresh of enterprise risks and a new Risk Management Framework 2025-27
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework intent to rebuild APS capability and limit reliance on contractors for core work

Challenges & risks

  • Budget position constraining delivery of the audit work program (a named enterprise risk)
  • Workforce attraction and retention in a globally competitive audit talent market, with high sector attrition
  • Security of the sensitive information the ANAO holds, and cyber resilience
  • Capacity to deliver new/emerging audit areas (climate disclosures, AI-enabled auditing)
  • Investing sufficiently in tools and technology to audit contemporarily while maintaining quality
  • Maintaining institutional independence and reputation (risk appetite stated as 'low')

Talking points

  • The plan names workforce attrition and talent competition as a standing risk — an independent, PGPA-fluent workforce and retention review can strengthen the 'grow our own' model without touching audit independence.
  • ANAO is building a digital strategy and AI policy this year; the Consulting Firm can provide independent investment-governance and data-governance advice (advisory, not systems build) to ensure the spend delivers measurable productivity and quality gains.
  • With funding unable to cover the JCPAA-recommended audit volume, an activity-based costing view of audit products could sharpen efficiency decisions and support the budget case to the Parliament.
  • We understand ANAO's independence and reputation sensitivities and would scope any engagement strictly to corporate/enabling functions, with explicit conflict-of-interest management.
  • The integration of financial and performance statements audits and the 'rapid review' approach are operating-model changes where independent process and change support can de-risk the transition.

Caveats: This is a deliberately enabling-focused plan from the Commonwealth's own audit and assurance body. Several the Consulting Firm service lines (Assurance, Governance/Risk, Evaluation/Performance) are precisely ANAO's own core expertise, so pitching them would be non-credible and is avoided here. ANAO's strong independence stance, 'low' reputation risk appetite, alignment with the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework (favouring in-house work), and small budget all constrain external advisory demand. The genuine, proportionate opportunities are modest, corporate in nature (workforce, digital/AI investment governance, costing), and would require careful conflict-of-interest management given ANAO audits the sector the Consulting Firm serves. Confidences are set accordingly low/medium; no named procurement is evident in the plan.
