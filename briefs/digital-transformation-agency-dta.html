Digital Transformation Agency (DTA)
Finance · Corporate Plan 2024-25 (plan text supplied covers 2024-25 to 2027-28; source URL references 2025-26) · BD confidence: Medium

The DTA is a small, expertise-heavy oversight and policy body in the Finance portfolio — it coordinates whole-of-government digital transformation but explicitly does not deliver it. Its agenda centres on co-delivering the Data and Digital Government Strategy, running the Investment Oversight Framework (IOF) and digital/ICT assurance, managing strategic sourcing through BuyICT/Digital Marketplace, leading AI assurance policy, and building its own workforce capability. The plan openly flags longer-term financial sustainability as a significant risk and the need for an improved operating model. Crucially, the DTA is committed to the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework and is actively reducing outsourcing of core work (including program/project management, accounting/finance and communications) — a genuine headwind for traditional consulting. The strongest external pressure is procurement: successive ANAO audits found its ICT procurement ineffective and its reform response only 'partly effective', keeping it under JCPAA and media scrutiny. BD should therefore concentrate on specialised, defensible niches — procurement assurance/remediation, financial-sustainability advice and cyber — rather than broad advisory.

Strategic priorities

  • Lead the Data and Digital Government Strategy — Co-deliver the DDGS and its Implementation Plan to 2030, providing policy design, strategy, advice and whole-of-government architecture, including realising the new Digital Experience (DX) Policy and standards.
  • Oversee the digital and ICT investment portfolio via the IOF — Run the Investment Oversight Framework across its six states (strategic planning, prioritisation, contestability, assurance, sourcing, operations), including assurance plans for in-scope investments and support to at-risk projects.
  • Manage whole-of-government strategic sourcing and BuyICT — Operate and improve the BuyICT platform and Digital Marketplace, including standing up Digital Marketplace Panel 2 and a new ERP category, and pre-negotiating contract terms for buyers and sellers.
  • Lead safe and responsible AI in government — Drive the National framework for the assurance of AI in government and the policy for responsible use of AI, mandatory for APS agencies from 1 September 2024, transitioning AI Taskforce functions into the DTA's strategy, policy, sourcing and assurance responsibilities.
  • Be a valued employer and build workforce capability — Develop a 3-year Workforce Plan to identify skills gaps and a path to a fit-for-purpose future state, while managing funding pressures and the agency's own digital transformation.

Reform & change drivers

  • Data and Digital Government Strategy (2023) and its 2030 vision of 'simple, secure and connected public services' driving whole-of-government uplift
  • New Digital Experience (DX) Policy and standards mandating requirements on agencies
  • Mandatory policy for responsible use of AI in government from 1 September 2024 and the National AI assurance framework
  • Investment Oversight Framework (IOF) and an expanded DTA role in advising on and assuring digital projects across government
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework requiring reduced outsourcing of core work (~$0.8m reduction targeted in 2024-25)
  • Establishment of Digital Marketplace Panel 2 (October 2024) and a new ERP marketplace category
  • Sustained ANAO and JCPAA scrutiny of DTA procurement compliance and reform implementation

Challenges & risks

  • Longer-term financial sustainability explicitly named as a significant risk; no sustainable funding/resource model to fully meet the mandate
  • Short-term funding pressures requiring enhanced financial management, forecasting and reporting
  • Risk that BuyICT and whole-of-government sourcing arrangements are not seen as delivering value for buyers and sellers
  • Risk the DTA's advice/IOF is not seen as relevant to effective digital and ICT investment decisions
  • Need to identify and implement an improved operating model and ways of working
  • Workforce/skills risk — building digital capability while reducing reliance on contractors under the Strategic Commissioning Framework
  • Persistent, externally documented procurement-compliance and value-for-money weaknesses (ANAO)

In the news & controversies

  • InnovationAus reported the DTA was criticised over ~$55m of ICT spending, with the audit citing conflicts of interest, suppliers given unfair advantages, above-market rates, duplicate payments, disregard of fraud warnings and poor records across nine procurements. (InnovationAus — https://www.innovationaus.com/dta-slammed-for-breaking-procurement-rules-in-55m-ict-spend/)
    Heightened procurement scrutiny means any engagement faces strict CPR-compliance and value-for-money tests — and signals demand for procurement governance and probity uplift.
  • The DTA launched a review of all significant procurements after the audit, having bypassed the Commonwealth Procurement Rules by sourcing directly from suppliers, including for the myGov upgrade contract. (InnovationAus — https://www.innovationaus.com/dta-reviews-all-significant-procurements-after-shocker-audit/)
    Direct/sole-source routes are now politically risky; expect more competitive, panel-based processes and demand for procurement strategy support.
  • A follow-up audit (Oct 2025) found the DTA's procurement-reform attempts only 'partly effective', still failing to demonstrate value for money and meet CPR obligations. (InnovationAus — https://www.innovationaus.com/dta-procurement-reforms-fall-short-in-follow-up-audit/)
    Procurement governance remains an unresolved, reputationally sensitive issue — a credible opportunity for procurement assurance, risk and capability uplift.

ANAO audit findings

  • Auditor-General Report No. 5 of 2022-23 found the DTA's procurement of ICT-related services 'ineffective'; none of nine procurements ($54.5m) fully complied with the CPRs, one directly-sourced contract increased ~40-fold with major scope changes, and nine recommendations were made. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/digital-transformation-agency-procurement-ict-related-services)
    Foundational document for any BD approach — named gaps (risk management, contract variation discipline, value-for-money) map directly to procurement and assurance service lines.
  • Auditor-General Report No. 3 of 2025-26 assessed implementation of 11 JCPAA/Auditor-General recommendations and concluded the DTA has been only 'partly effective'; improvements are not embedded and processes do not yet fully comply with the CPRs. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/implementation-of-procurement-reforms-dta-finance)
    A standing, audited remediation backlog signals near-term demand for reform implementation, monitoring frameworks and recommendation-closure support.
  • The ANAO forward work program lists planned/active audits touching the DTA, including administration of frameworks for emerging technologies in the public sector and digital identity / Digital ID Program reforms. (ANAO work program — https://www.anao.gov.au/work-program/portfolio/quick)
    Emerging-tech governance and Digital ID are flagged for future audit — areas where pre-emptive assurance and compliance advice will be valued.

Parliamentary matters

  • The Joint Committee of Public Accounts and Audit (JCPAA) conducted a procurement inquiry that considered the 2022-23 Auditor-General report on the DTA and recommended the follow-up audit that became Report No. 3 of 2025-26. (ANAO (describing the JCPAA inquiry) — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/implementation-of-procurement-reforms-dta-finance)
    Sustained parliamentary committee interest keeps DTA procurement under oversight, reinforcing demand for demonstrable compliance and reform delivery.
