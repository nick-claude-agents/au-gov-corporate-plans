National Blood Authority (NBA)
Health · Corporate Plan 2025-26 to 2028-29 · BD confidence: Medium

The NBA is a small (75 staff, ~$15m departmental) statutory agency that coordinates Australia's national blood arrangements and manages ~$2.2b in administered product supply contracts on behalf of all governments. Its archetype is a specialised regulator/coordinator: expertise-heavy, contract- and data-intensive, with limited large-scale delivery but several time-bound, high-value reform points over the plan period. The strongest latent demand sits in ICT/data modernisation (the blood sector systems are explicitly at or near end-of-life), a wave of major procurements (NaFAA expiry Dec 2026, a new Lifeblood Deed, commercial supplier contracts), and a live Commonwealth-initiated Review of the National Blood Arrangements that will drive governance and implementation change. Leadership transition (a new Chief Executive after nine years) and a refreshed ICT and Data Strategy create natural entry points. BD should right-size offerings to a small-budget agency while recognising the redevelopment of blood sector systems could sustain multi-year digital delivery if government funding is secured.

Strategic priorities

  • Modernise end-of-life blood sector ICT systems — The NBA states its blood sector ICT systems (e.g. BloodNet, ABDR, authorisations) are at or near end-of-life with complex architecture. It will implement a refreshed ICT Strategy, complete the cloud transition, and commence development of the next generation of blood sector systems while sustaining existing ones.
  • Major contract renewals and procurements — Several critical national agreements expire or are being renegotiated: the NaFAA with CSL Behring (expires 31 Dec 2026), a new Lifeblood Deed of Agreement (current Deed ends 30 June 2028, development commencing), revised Red Cell Diagnostic Products arrangements, plus ongoing modernised commercial supplier contracts.
  • Data strategy and demand/inventory analytics uplift — The NBA will finalise the NBA Data Strategy 2025-28, run a Data Improvement Program, develop data management/analysis/visualisation solutions, pursue hospital-level data access and supplier data sharing, and improve demand forecasting and inventory management.
  • Respond to the Commonwealth Review of National Blood Arrangements — The NBA will continue contributing to a Commonwealth-initiated review of the national blood arrangements through 2025-26 and prepare to implement its outcomes to keep the arrangements modern and fit for purpose.
  • Strengthen supply contingency and risk management — The NBA is reviewing and updating the National Blood Supply Contingency Plan, planning sector-wide exercises and simulations, and reviewing national blood sector risk management arrangements.
  • Workforce capability and corporate modernisation — Having converted contractor roles to APS roles, the NBA will strengthen leadership, contract management, procurement, digital and data capabilities, modernise corporate digital/business systems, and update records management.

Reform & change drivers

  • Commonwealth-initiated Review of the National Blood Arrangements running through 2025-26 with outcomes to be implemented in later years
  • Expiry of the National Fractionation Agreement for Australia (NaFAA) on 31 December 2026 requiring renegotiation of a critical plasma manufacturing arrangement
  • New Lifeblood Deed of Agreement to be developed (current Deed ends 30 June 2028)
  • Blood sector ICT systems at or near end-of-life requiring significant investment and redevelopment
  • Plasma Pathway (launched July 2025) and transition to Gender-Neutral Assessments changing donor eligibility and supply dynamics
  • Leadership transition — the Chief Executive is departing after nine years
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework driving reduction of external resources and conversion to APS roles
  • Rising global demand, supply pressures and immunoglobulin demand growth (8-9% per year) increasing cost and forecasting pressure

Challenges & risks

  • End-of-life ICT systems with complex architecture; securing government funding to modernise/redevelop is itself flagged as a risk
  • Volatile blood inventory levels and significant variability in product usage creating demand and cost uncertainty
  • Tight global market for immunoglobulin and imported products affecting price and availability
  • Changing donor behaviour affecting donor panel size and donations
  • Need for better information, data and analysis to support demand forecasting and supply decisions
  • Complexity of managing multiple suppliers for imported products
  • Enterprise risks named in the plan: interruption of supply, unsustainable supply costs, loss of confidence/reputation, major system compromise/failure, insufficient resources
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework constrains use of external resources — a headwind for any consulting engagement that must be navigated carefully

In the news & controversies

  • In January 2025 the Commonwealth, on behalf of all Australian governments, engaged an independent reviewer to examine the governance frameworks and administrative processes underpinning the national blood arrangements. (Department of Health, Disability and Ageing — https://www.health.gov.au/topics/blood-and-blood-products/what-were-doing-about-blood-and-blood-products)
    A live, government-commissioned governance/administrative review signals likely structural or process change at the NBA — a near-term opening for governance redesign, implementation and change-management work, and worth tracking for the review's recommendations and resulting procurement.
