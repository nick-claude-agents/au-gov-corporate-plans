Generated 05 June 2026 · Source corporate plan
Office of the Commonwealth Ombudsman
Attorney-General · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-26 to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Medium

The Office of the Commonwealth Ombudsman is a small, expertise-heavy integrity and oversight entity (regulator/oversight archetype) carrying 20 separate statutory functions. This year is dominated by two transformation pressures: replacing its ageing case management/CRM system (expected to roll out in 2025-26) and standing up and embedding the new National Student Ombudsman function (commenced 1 Feb 2025), which has driven significant expansion and new capability needs including alternative dispute resolution. It is doing this against a stated 'reducing funding envelope' and a Strategic Commissioning Framework that explicitly seeks to reduce outsourcing of core work, so BD must target enabling/corporate uplift (technology, workforce, governance, assurance, performance) rather than core complaint handling. The most actionable opportunities are the CRM build/change rollout, NSO establishment support, the Quality Management Framework and risk roadmap, workforce capability uplift, and the externally commissioned satisfaction research.

Strategic priorities

  • Improve individual complaint-handling performance — The Office openly admits complaint-handling performance is an area for improvement and has made improving its individual complaints service the central strategic priority for the year, including earlier resolution and expanded conciliation/restorative engagement.
  • Replace the ageing case management / CRM system — A significant, complex project commenced in 2024-25 to replace the legacy case management system that is integrated across all complaint-handling processes, with rollout anticipated in 2025-26 to enable more efficient handling and better complainant communication.
  • Establish and embed the National Student Ombudsman (NSO) — The NSO commenced 1 Feb 2025, providing 1.5 million higher education students an escalated complaints mechanism. It has required significant expansion, new internal capability, new ADR powers, and stakeholder/advisory group establishment, and will remain a major focus.
  • Embed the Quality Management Framework (QMF) — The Office is embedding its Quality Management Framework within all branches, working to fully implement it by early 2026.
  • Respond to AI and automated decision-making — AI/ADM is named as the key environmental factor, both as an oversight responsibility over agencies and as a tool the Office itself wants to harness, balanced against the risks of emerging technology.
  • Grow people and capability — Delivery of the 2024-28 Workforce Plan, including capability profiles by job family, career pathways, a succession planning framework, a Manager Essentials Development Program, an Employee Value Proposition and a Diversity, Inclusion and Belonging Strategy 2025-27.
  • Develop a risk roadmap — Following a review of its Risk Management Policy and Framework, the Office will develop a risk roadmap in 2025-26 to guide investment and enhance risk culture.

Reform & change drivers

  • Commencement of the National Student Ombudsman function (1 Feb 2025) with 1.5 million students in scope and new ADR powers
  • Legislative change imposing a statutory duty on secretaries/CEOs to assist Ombudsman investigations, plus new powers to access agency information systems
  • Rapid expansion of AI and automated decision-making across the APS, creating both oversight demand and internal adoption questions
  • Replacement of the legacy case management/CRM system
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework requiring reduced outsourcing of core work (labour hire at 11% vs a 5% target)
  • Reducing funding envelope identified as a strategic risk
  • Robodebt Royal Commission recommendation already influencing practice (communications log for Own Motion Investigations)
  • OPCAT/National Preventive Mechanism coordination obligations across jurisdictions

Challenges & risks

  • Admitted underperformance in complaint handling and ambitious timeliness sub-measures (e.g. 50% of complaints finalised in 30 working days)
  • Operating model and systems may not keep pace with AI, reducing service effectiveness (Strategic Risk 3)
  • Inability to deliver legislative obligations within a reducing funding envelope (Strategic Risk 5)
  • Breadth of 20 functions described as 'both an opportunity and a challenge' — stretched capacity
  • Workforce capacity/capability gaps; reliance on labour hire above target while recruiting; up to 3 months to make some roles operationally effective
  • Risk to impartiality, reputation and stakeholder confidence (Strategic Risks 1 and 2)
  • Cyber and data security of sensitive complaint information during system transition
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework constrains use of external providers for core work

    The CRM replacement is the single largest, time-bound initiative with a 2025-26 rollout; delivery, migration and change support are where an implementation-capable firm wins, and the corporate/enabling angle sidesteps the Strategic Commissioning Framework's core-work restriction.
  • High Engage the First Assistant Ombudsman (NSO) and NSO branch on establishment support — operating model, ADR/conciliation process design and PMO setup.
    The NSO is a brand-new at-scale function still embedding, with admitted capability-building needs; early advisory/design positioning can convert into broader delivery as the function matures.
  • Medium Offer the Audit and Risk Committee / Governance team a proposal on the risk roadmap and QMF implementation assurance.
    Both are explicit, named 2025-26 deliverables with near-term deadlines (QMF by early 2026), and assurance/governance work is the easiest independent-advisory win for an integrity body that prizes impartiality.
