Clean Energy Regulator (CER)
Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water · Corporate Plan 2025–29 (budget year 2025–26) · BD confidence: Low

The Clean Energy Regulator is a regulator-archetype entity administering Australia's key carbon and clean-energy schemes — the Australian Carbon Credit Unit (ACCU) scheme, the Emissions Reduction Fund, the Safeguard Mechanism and the Renewable Energy Target. The supplied 'corporate plan' content is only the publication landing page: it confirms a Corporate Plan 2025–29 was published on 29 August 2025 and lists its standard sections (purpose, operating-environment challenges, planning priorities/key activities, capability, strategic risks, performance), but contains none of the substantive narrative. Consequently the BD signals in this brief are inferred from CER's known mandate and the external research rather than from plan text. The dominant, well-evidenced demand driver is scheme integrity and assurance pressure following the Chubb Review, sustained public and academic criticism of ACCU integrity, and an ANAO 'largely effective' audit rating — all pointing to assurance, governance, data/MRV and evaluation work. This brief should be treated as provisional until the full plan PDF is analysed.

Strategic priorities

  • Administering and reforming the carbon market — CER's core ongoing activity is operating the ACCU scheme, Emissions Reduction Fund and registry, alongside implementing reforms flowing from the independent Chubb Review of ACCUs. Reform implementation (methods, transparency, registry uplift) is the most probable multi-year agenda.
  • Building capability to deliver future strategic direction — The plan explicitly commits to setting out 'how we'll build capabilities to deliver our future strategic direction' — a standard signal of workforce, data and systems investment for an expertise-heavy regulator.
  • Managing high-level strategic risks — The plan includes a section on 'high-level strategic risks we face in achieving our objectives' — for a contested scheme administrator these centre on integrity, market confidence and assurance of issued units.

Reform & change drivers

  • Implementation of the independent (Chubb) Review recommendations, including overhaul of ACCU methods, transparency and registry arrangements
  • Expanding regulatory remit across the Safeguard Mechanism reforms and a growing regulated population of large emitters
  • Sustained public and academic scrutiny of ACCU scheme integrity creating pressure for stronger measurement, reporting and verification (MRV)
  • Value-for-money scrutiny over ~$1bn already spent purchasing ACCUs with ~$1.6bn further contracted

Challenges & risks

  • Scheme integrity and public-confidence risk — high-profile allegations that a large share of ACCUs lack genuine abatement
  • Perceived conflict-of-interest in CER's combined role issuing credits and administering the fund, with public calls to break up the entity
  • ANAO 'largely effective' rating implying open recommendations and remediation work
  • Expertise and data-capability demands of a fast-evolving, technically complex regulatory environment

In the news & controversies

  • ANU Professor Andrew Macintosh, a former chair of the Emissions Reduction Assurance Committee, alleged up to 80% of ACCUs issued under three popular methods were 'devoid of integrity' and did not represent real abatement, calling the CER-administered scheme an 'environmental and taxpayer fraud'. (ANU Law School — https://law.anu.edu.au/news-and-events/news/australias-carbon-market-fraud-environment ; UNSW BusinessThink — https://www.businessthink.unsw.edu.au/articles/australia-carbon-credits-review)
    Integrity, independent verification and MRV reform are live demand areas; positioning around scheme credibility and independent assurance is well aligned.
  • The same ANU analysis recommended breaking up the Clean Energy Regulator, repealing 'low integrity' methods and an independent review, arguing the body has a conflicted combined role. (ANU Law Reform & Social Justice — https://lrsj.anu.edu.au/australias-carbon-market-a-fraud-on-the-environment/ ; RenewEconomy — https://reneweconomy.com.au/a-fraud-on-the-environment-whistleblower-slams-australias-carbon-offset-regime/)
    Governance and structural/conflict-of-interest advisory may be relevant; the entity's mandate and structure are publicly contested.
  • CER publicly rejected the allegations, stating no substantial evidence had been provided and that it was 'dismayed' by the attribution of those outcomes to its work. (UNSW BusinessThink — https://www.businessthink.unsw.edu.au/articles/australia-carbon-credits-review)
    The entity is sensitive to integrity criticism; any approach should be constructive and evidence-based, not adversarial.
  • The independent Chubb Review concluded it did not share critics' integrity concerns but nonetheless recommended a major overhaul of the scheme. (The Canberra Times — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/8041355/)
    Implementation of Chubb-driven reforms (methods, transparency, registry) is a probable pipeline of program and assurance work.
  • Critics highlighted ~$1bn spent purchasing ACCUs with a further ~$1.6bn contracted, framing continued purchases under contested methods as poor value for money. (ANU Law School — https://law.anu.edu.au/news-and-events/news/australias-carbon-market-fraud-environment)
    Value-for-money / procurement assurance and program-evaluation capability are relevant given scrutiny of spend.

ANAO audit findings

  • May 2024 reporting described an ANAO performance audit finding the carbon regulator 'largely effective' in administering the scheme. (Beef Central — https://www.beefcentral.com/carbon/audit-finds-carbon-regulator-to-be-largely-effective/)
    A 'largely effective' (not fully effective) rating typically signals open recommendations and remediation — a natural entry point for assurance and governance advisory. Audit title/number and recommendations should be confirmed directly at anao.gov.au.

Parliamentary matters

  • Secondary reporting indicates the Greens referred the carbon credit scheme to a watchdog following the whistleblower allegations, signalling parliamentary/political engagement with CER's programs. (RenewEconomy — https://reneweconomy.com.au/a-fraud-on-the-environment-whistleblower-slams-australias-carbon-offset-regime/)
    Ongoing parliamentary scrutiny raises the value of independent assurance and transparency uplift; a fuller aph.gov.au search (Estimates, committee inquiry, QoN) is needed to confirm specifics.
