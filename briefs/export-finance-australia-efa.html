Export Finance Australia (EFA)
Foreign Affairs and Trade · Corporate Plan 2025-26 to 2028-29 (budget year 2025-26) · BD confidence: Low

EFA is the Commonwealth's export credit agency — a corporate Commonwealth entity with own-source revenue that finances Australian export trade, overseas investment and infrastructure with Australian benefit. Its 2025-26 plan is organised around a single strategic priority, 'Sustainable growth', delivered through five goals (drive commercial growth, strengthen key markets, deliver our mandates, ensure sustainability, accelerate digitalisation) and four enablers (people and culture; technology and systems; partners and stakeholders; communication). The clearest BD signals are the explicit 'accelerate digitalisation' goal (technology/systems modernisation), the 'ensure sustainability' goal (financial-sustainability and futureproofing of a commercially-funded entity), and persistent external scrutiny over ESG/fossil-fuel financing and mandate-effectiveness. As a small, expertise-heavy, commercially-oriented entity it suits proportionate advisory, assurance, digital-delivery, data and governance work rather than large transformation programs. The published plan text is thin (a landing page rather than the full PDF), so several opportunities are inferred from goal headings and external research.

Strategic priorities

  • Sustainable growth (overarching priority) — EFA's single stated strategic priority is to increase commercial finance to support more customers and futureproof the organisation — signalling a deliberate push to grow the loan/insurance book while keeping the entity financially and operationally durable.
  • Drive commercial growth — Grow commercial financing volumes and customer numbers, implying scaled origination, risk assessment and portfolio-management capability.
  • Strengthen key markets — Deepen EFA's presence and effectiveness in priority markets (likely including the Indo-Pacific and infrastructure financing such as the AIFFP).
  • Deliver our mandates — Execute EFA's expanding government mandates (e.g. overseas infrastructure financing, critical minerals, defence export, Pacific facility) effectively and demonstrably.
  • Ensure sustainability — Maintain financial and operational sustainability — managing risk, capital and ESG considerations so growth does not erode resilience.
  • Accelerate digitalisation — Modernise technology and systems to enable faster, more scalable customer service and operations — named as both a goal and an enabler.

Reform & change drivers

  • Expansion and execution of multiple government mandates (overseas infrastructure financing under the 2019 powers, AIFFP, and more recently critical minerals and defence export facilities), creating mandate-effectiveness and benefits-realisation demand.
  • Explicit 'accelerate digitalisation' goal and 'technology and systems' enabler, signalling system modernisation and digital service investment.
  • Growth ambition ('increase commercial finance to support more customers') requiring scalable origination, credit-risk and portfolio capability.
  • ESG/climate scrutiny over fossil-fuel versus renewables financing, pushing uplift in environmental & social transaction screening and disclosure.
  • Alignment with broader policy agendas (e.g. Future Made in Australia, Clean Energy Transition Partnership) shaping the financing portfolio.

Challenges & risks

  • Reputational and policy pressure from sustained NGO campaigns on fossil-fuel financing and transparency.
  • Transparency/accountability criticism (Budget 'not for publication' items; partial FOI exemption) creating disclosure-uplift pressure.
  • Mandate-implementation risk — an independent review found it 'too early' to assess effectiveness of the 2019 infrastructure powers, implying ongoing evaluation need.
  • Balancing commercial growth with prudent credit/portfolio risk as the book expands.
  • Legacy or under-scaled technology limiting the 'accelerate digitalisation' ambition.
  • Regular Senate Estimates and ANAO scrutiny of value-for-money and outcomes across its facilities and AIFFP arrangements.

In the news & controversies

  • A coalition (Jubilee Australia, Oxfam, the Australia Institute, ACF) campaigned for EFA to stop financing coal, oil and gas; Jubilee research alleged EFA provided roughly 80x more finance to fossil fuels than renewables (~$1.69bn vs ~$20m, 2009–2020), and EFA was named among financiers ruled out of the Papua LNG project. (Jubilee Australia — https://www.jubileeaustralia.org/news/latest-news/category/efa)
    Live ESG/reputational pressure point — advisory on environmental & social transaction screening, E&S policy and disclosure/transparency is valued and politically sensitive.
  • Per a Wikipedia summary citing the Australia Institute, the 2020-21 Budget set a record for 'not for publication' items, with export-finance-related expenditure hidden, prompting accountability concerns (note: 2020-21, slightly outside the 3-year window). (Wikipedia (citing the Australia Institute) — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Export_Finance_Australia)
    Disclosure/accountability is a recurring critique — proposals demonstrating transparency uplift and accountable reporting may resonate, though FOI sensitivities apply.
  • In Nov 2021 the Government tabled an independent review by Stephen Sedgwick AO of EFA's 2019 overseas infrastructure-financing powers; it made 25 findings, noted seven transactions (~$144m) would not have proceeded without the new mandate, and concluded it was 'too early' to assess effectiveness of the 2019 amendments. (DFAT — https://www.dfat.gov.au/news/news/export-finance-australia-independent-review-overseas-infrastructure-financing-powers)
    A documented evidence base flagging early-stage mandate implementation — useful framing for program-evaluation, benefits-realisation and mandate-effectiveness advisory.

ANAO audit findings

  • ANAO conducted a performance audit, 'Effectiveness of the Export Finance and Insurance Corporation' (Auditor-General Report No. 44 of 2018-19) — older than the 3-year window. (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/effectiveness-export-finance-and-insurance-corporation)
    Provides a documented baseline of audit expectations on effectiveness; a fresh audit may be overdue, signalling future readiness/assurance demand.
  • An ANAO performance audit on DFAT's establishment of the Australian Infrastructure Financing Facility for the Pacific (AIFFP) is scoped to examine DFAT's management of its arrangements with EFA; AIFFP comprises up to $500m grants plus up to $1.5bn loans over seven years (established July 2019). (ANAO — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/dfats-establishment-the-australian-infrastructure-financing-facility-the-pacific-0)
    EFA's AIFFP delivery role is an audit focus area — assurance, controls and governance-of-arrangements advisory could be in demand around this facility.

Parliamentary matters

  • EFA's senior management attends Senate Estimates three times a year (Foreign Affairs, Defence and Trade committee) to answer for its operations, the Minister responds to parliamentary questions/orders, and EFA's annual reports are tabled in Parliament. (APH Senate Estimates (FADT) — https://www.aph.gov.au/Parliamentary_Business/Senate_estimates/fadt)
    EFA faces regular parliamentary scrutiny — engagements should be framed to withstand Estimates-level questioning on value-for-money and outcomes.
  • A 2019 Senate committee inquiry examined the Export Finance and Insurance Corporation Amendment (Support for Infrastructure Financing) Bill 2019, which expanded EFA's mandate (note: 2019, outside the window). (DFAT — https://www.dfat.gov.au/news/news/export-finance-australia-independent-review-overseas-infrastructure-financing-powers)
    Mandate-expansion scrutiny is documented — relevant background for policy, legislative-implementation and stakeholder-engagement advisory.
