Australian Office of Financial Management (AOFM)
Treasury · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-26 to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Medium

The AOFM is Australia's sovereign debt manager — a very small (~44-52 staff), expertise-heavy non-corporate entity that issues Australian Government Securities, manages the Commonwealth's cash and debt portfolios, and runs the ABSF and Green Treasury Bond programs. Its 2025-26 plan is fundamentally conservative (zero-failure settlement, prudent debt strategy), but it openly flags an organisational restructure, its first-ever strategic workforce plan, a new Consultation and Change Framework, brand/communications uplift, and risk-maturity improvements. External research reveals an acute governance and culture crisis: a spike in corruption-perception census results, ~50% staff turnover, a contested restructure with ~$598k in redundancies, and a Treasury-commissioned independent review published April 2026. The combination of admitted workforce/change priorities in the plan and a live external review creates genuine but politically sensitive advisory demand, concentrated in workforce, organisational design, governance/integrity, and change management — proportionate to a tiny agency that performs most work in-house.

Strategic priorities

  • Organisational restructure and senior-leadership uplift — The agency is undertaking an organisational restructure and has expanded its senior leadership tier to sharpen strategic focus, accountability and coordination across functions.
  • First strategic workforce plan — In 2025-26 the AOFM will develop and begin implementing its first strategic workforce plan, focused on knowing, retaining and attracting its workforce in a highly competitive labour market for specialist debt-capital-markets skills.
  • Embed change management — The agency intends to embed a new Consultation and Change Framework and develop internal/external communications plans to improve consistency and effectiveness of change.
  • Increase brand awareness and stakeholder engagement — Develop a communications plan to lift the AOFM's profile with other entities, Central Borrowing Authorities, investors and policy agencies, and to develop a 'Team Australia' investor narrative.
  • Uplift risk-management maturity — The agency has a program of iterative improvements to lift risk-management maturity over the medium term, including climate-risk assessments, controls monitoring and business-continuity integration to inform its assurance program.
  • Knowledge management and continuity of integral functions — Addressing the risk that weak knowledge-transfer mechanisms erode future workforce capability by documenting integral functions and identifying critical roles.

Reform & change drivers

  • Organisational restructure and expanded senior leadership tier currently underway
  • Development of a first strategic workforce plan in a highly competitive labour market
  • New Consultation and Change Framework being embedded
  • Reliance on Treasury for ICT and on third-party finance applications (Tradeweb, Quantum, Austraclear), driving cyber-security and assurance obligations
  • Medium-term risk-maturity uplift program including climate risk, controls monitoring and business continuity
  • Growth in sovereign green bond / sustainable finance market and the Green Treasury Bond program
  • Higher AGS issuance expected in 2025-26 amid elevated geopolitical and market volatility
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework — commitment to perform most core work in-house with minimal outsourcing (constrains external demand)

Challenges & risks

  • Talent attraction and retention in a highly competitive labour market for specialist skills (named strategic opportunity/risk)
  • Loss of institutional knowledge if knowledge-transfer mechanisms are weak (named strategic risk)
  • Dependence on Treasury-provided ICT and a small number of critical third-party suppliers whose compromise would severely impact operations
  • Cyber-security exposure across third-party finance-sector applications
  • Reputational and credibility risk if market confidence is lost or settlements fail
  • Severe staff turnover (~50% reported externally) and a contested restructure threatening operational continuity
  • Integrity/culture concerns evidenced by elevated corruption-perception census results the CEO could not explain

In the news & controversies

  • In the 2025 APS census, 18% of AOFM staff reported observing corrupt behaviour — reportedly double the 2024 figure — and the CEO told Senate Estimates she could not explain the result. (The Canberra Times — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9127416/office-of-financial-management-staff-raise-corruption-concerns/)
    A live integrity/culture problem the agency head cannot account for signals near-term demand for governance, integrity-framework, fraud-control and culture-review services.
  • At least 22 staff reportedly left the extra-small agency in just over a year (leaving ~52), with more than a quarter of remaining staff indicating they wished to leave within a year. (The Canberra Times — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9172097/treasury-agency-faces-mass-staff-exodus-and-review/)
    Severe retention loss in a high-stakes function points to demand for workforce strategy, change management and continuity support — while also raising delivery risk from lost institutional knowledge.
  • FOI documents showed the CEO requested an independent review of her own agency amid staff concerns about leadership, high turnover and a December 2025 restructure; ~$598,000 was spent on redundancies to five staff and ~19 staff left in the year to February. (The Canberra Times — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9231270/anna-hughes-requested-aofm-review-amid-staff-disquiet/)
    A formally commissioned external review plus a contested restructure typically generates downstream advisory, organisational-design and implementation work — but engagements will be politically charged and closely watched.
  • Treasury established and published an independent review of the AOFM (Terms of Reference Feb 2026; review published ~24 April 2026). The review's specific findings could not be retrieved during research. (Treasury — https://treasury.gov.au/review/aofm (review: https://treasury.gov.au/publication/p2026-764908; ToR: https://treasury.gov.au/publication/p2026-742432))
    The review is the single most important near-term signal of where reform funding and follow-on work will flow; its recommendations should be read in full before any approach.

Parliamentary matters

  • The CEO appeared before the Senate Economics Legislation Committee (Treasury portfolio) and was questioned on the elevated corruption-census figures, stating she was 'still trying to get to the bottom of them'; updated staffing, turnover and restructure details were also provided to a Senate committee in February 2026. (The Canberra Times — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9127416/office-of-financial-management-staff-raise-corruption-concerns/ and https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9231270/anna-hughes-requested-aofm-review-amid-staff-disquiet/)
    AOFM's culture, turnover and restructure are now on the parliamentary record and likely to recur at Estimates, meaning any engagement carries scrutiny risk and must be designed to withstand committee questioning.
