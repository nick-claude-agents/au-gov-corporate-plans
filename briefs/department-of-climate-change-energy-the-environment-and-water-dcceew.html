Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water (DCCEEW)
Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

DCCEEW is a large, multi-mandate policy and regulatory department (~5,790 staff) delivering an exceptionally broad reform agenda spanning the energy transition (net zero by 2050, 82% renewables by 2030, Capacity Investment Scheme), wholesale reform of Australia's environmental laws (EPBC reform, standing up a national Environment Protection Agency), the Murray-Darling Basin Plan and an additional 450 GL water recovery target, Antarctic operations, and the National Climate Risk Assessment/National Adaptation Plan. The plan signals heavy reform delivery pressure, a recent and still-stabilising ICT separation from shared services (first full year as a self-managed ICT provider in 2025-26), and an explicit push to bring core work in-house under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework — which tempers some advisory demand but creates clear scope for specialist delivery, assurance and data work. Numerous new and reworked performance measures, a maturing enterprise risk and integrity agenda, and multiple named cross-jurisdictional programs make this a strong prospect for delivery assurance, performance/evaluation, data and digital, and program-delivery support. The Strategic Commissioning Framework insourcing intent is the key commercial caveat: position around specialist capability, build/implementation and independent assurance rather than generalist staff augmentation.

Strategic priorities

  • Net Zero Plan and sectoral decarbonisation — Lead development of a new Net Zero Plan underpinned by six sectoral decarbonisation plans, set the 2035 emissions target (next NDC), and lead the Electricity and Energy Sector Plan while supporting other sector plans.
  • Energy market transformation and investment — Drive renewable investment via the Capacity Investment Scheme (target uplifted to 40 GW), Renewable Energy Transformation Agreements with states, offshore wind framework, Rewiring the Nation and consumer energy programs while maintaining security, reliability and affordability.
  • Environmental law reform and a national EPA — Reform Australia's environmental laws to streamline assessments and speed up decisions, and progress arrangements to establish Australia's first national Environment Protection Agency.
  • Climate adaptation and resilience — Release and implement the National Climate Risk Assessment and National Adaptation Plan, including designing a monitoring, evaluation and learning (MEL) system, and run the whole-of-government Climate Risk and Opportunity Management Program.
  • Murray-Darling Basin Plan delivery and water reform — Deliver the Basin Plan including the additional 450 GL of environmental water by 31 December 2027, conduct the statutory review of the Water Act 2007, finalise a new National Water Agreement, and advance the Aboriginal Water Entitlements Program.
  • Nature positive, 30 by 30 and Nature Repair Market — Protect 30% of land and sea by 2030, implement the $250m Australian Bushland Program, deliver the Threatened Species Action Plan and Sustainable Ocean Plan, and unlock private capital through the Nature Repair Market.
  • Circular economy transition — Implement the Circular Economy Framework to double Australia's circularity by 2035, set standards and targeted regulation, and lift national resource recovery toward 80% by 2030.
  • ICT self-management, data and AI uplift — Stabilise, secure and rationalise newly self-managed ICT after exiting shared services, migrate off legacy systems to cloud, deliver an AI-enabled digital experience, and mature the data function under the Enterprise Data Strategy.

Reform & change drivers

  • Legislated emissions targets (43% by 2030, net zero by 2050) and the obligation to submit a new 2035 NDC under the Paris Agreement.
  • Water Amendment (Restoring Our Rivers) Act 2023 mandating 450 GL additional environmental water by 31 December 2027 and a statutory review of the Water Act 2007.
  • Environmental law reform program and creation of a national Environment Protection Agency (machinery-of-government change).
  • Release of the National Climate Risk Assessment and National Adaptation Plan, plus whole-of-government Climate Risk and Opportunity Management Program with mandatory climate risk reporting.
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework driving insourcing of core functions (Accounting/Finance, ICT, Data/Research, Policy, Communications, HR, Compliance, Science).
  • Exit from ICT shared services into self-managed ICT, with legacy-system replacement and cloud migration underway.
  • Capacity Investment Scheme target uplift to 40 GW and rollout of offshore wind, hydrogen and low-carbon liquid fuels frameworks.
  • Increasing climate hazard severity and economic exposure driving demand for trusted climate risk information.
  • Significant new and amended PGPA performance measures introduced to reflect a changing operating context.

Challenges & risks

  • Enterprise risk that the department cannot develop, deliver and implement effective regulatory practice or influential, integrated policy and programs across an unusually broad mandate.
  • Attraction, development and retention of staff with the right capabilities (named enterprise risk), compounded by insourcing targets that increase in-house delivery load.
  • Newly self-managed ICT environment requiring stabilisation, legacy-system replacement and cyber uplift — explicit enterprise risk on security/availability of data and information systems.
  • Climate-related risks to the department's own operations and achievement of purposes (enterprise risk 5), including APS Net Zero 2030 commitments.
  • Delivery of the 450 GL water recovery target by a hard statutory deadline (31 Dec 2027) with much depending on Basin states.
  • Achievement of 30 by 30 and emissions targets depends heavily on states/territories and factors outside the department's direct control.
  • Integrity and culture pressures evidenced by the Safe to Speak Up campaign and the Independent Review of Workplace Culture and Change at the Australian Antarctic Division.
  • Many performance measures rely on external data, modelling and case-study/qualitative approaches, creating attribution and evidence challenges.
