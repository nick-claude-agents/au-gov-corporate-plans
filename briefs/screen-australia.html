Screen Australia — Business Development Brief

Generated 05 June 2026 · Source corporate plan
Screen Australia
Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications, Sport & Arts · Corporate Plan 2025-29 (budget year 2025-26) · BD confidence: Medium

Screen Australia is a corporate Commonwealth entity (cultural/funding-body archetype) that has just adopted a wholly new strategic framework — five pillars (Empower, Enrich, Enable, Engage, Elevate) supported by ten priorities, twelve deliverables and an entirely refreshed performance-measurement set. The plan is candid about three operational gaps the firm can act on: a near-greenfield data/digital position (baseline 'No integration' of application, program and financial datasets), a brand-new performance framework where baselines are still being gathered over the next 12 months, and an explicit drive for operational efficiency to push more money to programs. It is also building new funding frameworks (e.g. enterprise/scale-up funding, a unified industry 'North Star'), simplifying its application process and investing in cloud systems and automation. Budgets are modest and funding pressure is a named risk, so opportunities must be right-sized to short advisory, framework-design and proportionate digital/data engagements rather than large multi-year programs. The strongest, most time-bound demand sits in Digital & ICT Delivery, Data/Analytics, and performance-framework/evaluation design.

Strategic priorities

  • Data-driven, effectiveness-focused decision making — A deliberate pivot from measuring volume of activity to measuring impact and effectiveness, underpinned by a new performance-measurement set with baselines still being established over the next 12 months.
  • Digital enablement and data integration — Moving from no integration of application, program and financial datasets to fully integrated data, plus investment in cloud-based systems, improved data management and automation to speed funding decisions and strengthen digital resilience.
  • Operational excellence and funding efficiency — Streamlining ways of working, removing duplication and simplifying the application process to direct more funding to programs and reduce delays.
  • New enterprise/business funding frameworks — Establishing a new funding framework to support commercially sustainable production businesses (scale-up, diversified revenue) rather than just individual projects.
  • Industry 'North Star' — a unified industry vision — Developing, with the Board and industry across FY2026, a unified industry vision to drive collaboration, investment and long-term growth, to be incorporated into performance tracking once established.
  • Global positioning and co-production growth — Lifting international investment in Australian productions, post-financing acquisitions and festival presence, including a co-production development program with international partners.

Reform & change drivers

  • Alignment to the National Cultural Policy (Revive) and the 2025-26 Portfolio Budget Statement, mapping each pillar to Revive's policy pillars.
  • A comprehensive internal review of resources, activities and performance metrics that produced the new strategic framework.
  • Industry transformation — technological change, global content flows, streaming/platform shifts and evolving audience expectations.
  • Insights and baselines from the 2025 Screen Industry Survey driving new measures and consultation.
  • Administration of the Producer Offset and the tension between offset requirements and changing distribution/business models.

Challenges & risks

  • Reduction in funding from the Australian Government against rising demand for limited funds.
  • Many new performance measures have no baseline yet ('-'), risking weak first-year performance reporting.
  • Digital baseline is 'No integration' of application, program and financial datasets — a significant data/systems gap the agency admits.
  • Screen investment recoupment targets not being met.
  • Discrepancy between Producer Offset requirements and changing business models, plus historical concern about inflated fees claimed under the offset.
  • Insufficient crew or infrastructure to meet production demand; increased litigation risk from competition for limited funds.
