Inspector-General of Intelligence and Security (IGIS)
Prime Minister and Cabinet · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Low

IGIS is a very small, expertise-heavy statutory oversight agency that provides independent assurance on the legality and propriety of Australia's six intelligence agencies (plus network-activity-warrant oversight of ACIC and AFP). Its 2025-26 plan is dominated by three pressures: a likely expansion of its jurisdiction (ISLAB and the agreed recommendation to bring all of ACIC under IGIS), acute recruitment and retention difficulties compounded by lengthy security-clearance timeframes, and the need to lift technical/AI literacy to keep pace with the agencies it oversees. Note the plan sits in the Attorney-General's portfolio per the cooperation section, despite the brief's PM&C label, and IGIS depends on shared services from the Attorney-General's Department. The addressable consulting market is genuinely narrow: core oversight work is done only by cleared APS officers, and high-clearance constraints limit external delivery. The most credible BD entry points are corporate/enabling functions — workforce strategy, information governance, risk-framework uplift and operating-model design for an expanding remit — kept proportionate to a small-agency budget.

Strategic priorities

  • Sustain rigorous oversight amid an expanding intelligence community — IGIS's primary focus is effective oversight in an environment of growing agency size, high intelligence tempo and technological change, while optimising its own activities to remain focused, efficient and proportionate.
  • Prepare for an expanded jurisdiction — The lapsed ISLAB and the government's agreed recommendation to have IGIS oversee the entirety of ACIC (in lieu of the Commonwealth Ombudsman) signal a coming expansion of remit that will drive activity and require new capacity and processes.
  • Recruit and retain specialised, security-cleared workforce — IGIS is implementing new and innovative HR strategies, an Employee Value Proposition, talent acquisition and succession planning to address acute skills shortages and clearance-related onboarding delays.
  • Build technical and AI literacy for oversight — Advances in technology and increasing AI use by overseen agencies require IGIS to develop staff technical literacy and ensure technical expertise (currently via a seconded Technical Advisor) is available to inform oversight.
  • Handle increasingly complex complaints and PIDs — Complaint volumes are stable but newer complaints from current/former staff demand more resources and require managing the complex interaction between the IGIS Act and the PID Act.

Reform & change drivers

  • Intelligence Services Legislation Amendment Bill 2023 (ISLAB) lapsed at prorogation; potential reintroduction would significantly expand IGIS jurisdiction to ACIC, AUSTRAC intelligence functions, AFP and Home Affairs intelligence functions
  • Government's November 2024 agreement to have IGIS oversee the entirety of ACIC (replacing the Commonwealth Ombudsman) following the independent review of the ACIC
  • 2024 Independent Intelligence Review affirming the oversight model as fit for purpose but noting continued growth of the intelligence community
  • Proposed reforms to the Public Interest Disclosure Act 2013 and the need to manage IGIS Act / PID Act interactions
  • Significant investment and growth in capacity and capability across the overseen intelligence agencies, increasing oversight workload
  • Increasing use of artificial intelligence and advanced technology by overseen agencies
  • Anticipated growth in strategic risks driven by an expanding workforce and evolving jurisdiction

Challenges & risks

  • Persistent recruitment and retention difficulties in a tight national-security labour market with strong competition for cyber, intelligence and strategic-policy talent
  • Lengthy external security-clearance processes causing delays between selection and commencement of staff
  • Small-agency vulnerability: loss of key, experienced staff poses outsized risk
  • Rising complexity and resource intensity of complaints, particularly from current/former intelligence staff
  • Maintaining sufficient technical/AI literacy to credibly oversee technologically advanced agencies
  • Strategic risks expected to grow with expanding workforce and jurisdiction
  • Dependence on shared-services arrangements with the Attorney-General's Department for ICT mobility and some systems
  • Bringing records and information governance into line with National Archives / Commonwealth requirements across electronic and hardcopy holdings
  • Qualitative-only performance framework limits demonstrable, quantitative evidence of effectiveness

  • Your information-governance roadmap and recordkeeping digitisation can be advanced with an information strategy designed for a highly classified environment and National Archives compliance.
