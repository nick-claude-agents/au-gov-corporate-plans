NDIS Quality and Safeguards Commission
Health, Disability and Ageing · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: High

The NDIS Commission is a relatively young, expertise-heavy regulator (archetype: regulator) overseeing ~263,000 providers and ~717,000 participants in a high-growth, high-scrutiny market. Its 2025-26 plan is dominated by three time-bound, multi-year change agendas: the $160.7m four-year Data and Regulatory Transformation (DART) program, a risk-based regulatory model to be fully implemented by end of 2025-26, and a significant regulatory reform package (mandatory registration for SIL/platform/support-coordination providers, Practice Standards review). The Commission is also under real pressure on culture (two independent culture/psychosocial reviews in 2024-25), capability/workforce confidence, financial sustainability, and external accountability (an ANAO performance audit of its regulatory functions tabled Sept 2025 and a Joint Standing Committee report with a government response). These combine to create credible, traceable demand across program delivery, assurance, data/ICT, workforce and governance — sized to a mid-small regulator budget rather than a large delivery agency.

Strategic priorities

  • Become a 'formidable' risk-based regulator — Shift from reactive case handling to a proactive, intelligence-led model. The Commission will fully implement its Risk-based Regulatory Model before end of 2025-26, referring more low/moderate-risk matters to providers and concentrating effort on serious/systemic risk.
  • Deliver the DART transformation — Replace outdated systems and uplift data and intelligence capability through a four-year program funded by a $160.7m allocation in the 2024-25 Budget, starting with a registration portal, complaints process streamlining and a single view of providers.
  • Regulatory reform of provider registration — Progress mandatory registration changes for supported independent living (SIL), platform providers and support coordinators, plan-manager requirements, and a review of NDIS Rules and Practice Standards to strengthen who enters and exits the market.
  • Reduce and eliminate restrictive practices — A standing regulatory priority backed by reworked performance measures (BSP quality, URP timeframes, reductions across plans), working with states and territories on authorisation.
  • Rebuild internal culture and workforce capability — Respond to an independent Culture Review (final report Aug 2025) and the Comcare Psychosocial Inspection (report expected late 2025) via a Culture Roadmap, Workforce Plan, capability uplift, and a Disability Action Plan.
  • Lift engagement and accessibility — Refresh website/intranet, deliver a contact centre upgrade (phase II) and publish a Markets Insights Report to be more accessible to participants, providers and the community.

Reform & change drivers

  • First tranche of NDIS Act reforms (Getting the NDIS Back on Track No. 1 Act 2024) — new Commissioner rule-making powers over approved quality auditors and greater delegation of compliance/enforcement powers.
  • Machinery-of-government change (May 2025) moving the Commission into the Department of Health, Disability and Ageing, plus two new responsible ministers.
  • Responses to the Disability Royal Commission and the NDIS Review (both late 2023) on fraud, scheme cost and violence/abuse/neglect/exploitation.
  • NDIS Provider and Worker Registration Taskforce advising on a risk-proportionate regulatory model.
  • Care and support economy growth ($21bn projected investment increase to 2028) and a regulatory harmonisation agenda across disability, aged care, early childhood and veterans' care.
  • $160.7m DART funding driving a four-year systems and data modernisation program.
  • Financial sustainability risk — the Commission flags it may not be appropriated at a sustainable level (enterprise risk R3).
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework — conversion of labour hire to APS staff, with a <10% labour-hire target but a short-term DART-driven increase in external technical expertise.

Challenges & risks

  • Regulatory effectiveness risk (R1) — that regulatory functions fail to shape a ~263,000-provider market with ~717,000 participants.
  • People/culture risk (R2) — immaturity in structures, policies and WHS systems supporting an engaged workforce, compounded by two independent culture/psychosocial reviews.
  • Financial sustainability risk (R3) — uncertain, possibly insufficient appropriation against a growing remit.
  • Fragmented ICT (R4) — legacy systems creating data blind spots and cyber/legislative-breach exposure, mitigated by DART.
  • Enterprise reform risk (R5) — strategic projects and reform not effectively coordinated, with poor change management and stakeholder confusion.
  • Shared risk (R6) — dependencies on DHDA and NDIA as co-stewards where external risk owners may not adequately oversee shared responsibilities.
  • New/immature performance measures — measure 6.3 is only baselining in 2025-26, and several restrictive-practice measures were redefined, signalling an evolving and unproven performance framework.

  • The ANAO regulatory-effectiveness audit and the Joint Standing Committee report give the Commission a defined remediation agenda; independent, gateway-style assurance shows Parliament and the minister the response is on track.
  • Two independent reviews (Culture and Comcare psychosocial) will produce recommendations in 2025 — a workforce and culture-roadmap implementation partner turns findings into delivered change.
  • A single view of providers and the Markets Insights Report need a data strategy and analytics backbone — we can build the platforms and the intelligence layer, not just advise.
  • Several performance measures are new or only baselining in 2025-26; a PGPA-aligned measure redesign and an evaluation of the risk-based model would strengthen the next annual performance statement.
