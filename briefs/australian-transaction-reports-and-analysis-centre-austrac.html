Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre (AUSTRAC)
Home Affairs · Corporate Plan 2025-29 · BD confidence: Medium

AUSTRAC is a regulator-plus-financial-intelligence-unit archetype undertaking the most significant change in its history: implementing the November 2024 AML/CTF reforms and onboarding 'Tranche 2' sectors (lawyers, accountants, real estate, dealers in precious metals/stones) that will lift its regulated population from over 19,000 to over 100,000 by mid-2026. This is paired with an explicit ambition to 'transform into a world-leading data-driven digital agency', a newly stood-up Data Division and Chief Data and Analytics Officer, an expanded enterprise portfolio management office (EPMO), and a substantial ICT modernisation program (registration/reporting portal, CRM, contact centre, case management, corporate platforms). A FATF mutual evaluation across 2025-2027 raises the stakes on demonstrable effectiveness, and AUSTRAC has just overhauled its performance measures, committing to establish baselines in 2025-26. The combination of legislative reform delivery, digital/data uplift, workforce growth and external evaluation creates clear, time-bound latent demand for program delivery, assurance, data, digital build, and stakeholder-engagement support, tempered by a Strategic Commissioning Framework preference to keep core work in-house.

Strategic priorities

  • Implement AML/CTF reforms and embed Tranche 2 sectors — Successfully implement the Government's AML/CTF reforms and bring an estimated 80,000+ new reporting entities (real estate, lawyers, accountants, dealers in precious metals/stones) into the regime to a global standard by 1 July 2026, including guidance, education and 'starter kits' for small businesses.
  • Transform into a data-driven, digital-first agency — Uplift core ICT and digital capability (architecture, information management, service management, cloud), modernise the reporting portal, CRM, contact centre and case management, and strengthen data, analytics and AI capability via the new Data Division.
  • Direct effort to highest risk and harm — Focus regulatory and intelligence resources on areas of highest risk and harm as defined by the NRA, NIC missions and SOCCC priorities, via six new outcome-oriented regulatory priorities for 2025-26.
  • Strengthen partnerships and the Fintel Alliance — Enhance and leverage strategic partnerships, including the expanded Fintel Alliance (now a distinct branch) and Pacific/regional capability-building, for mutual community protection.
  • Cultivate a united, high-performing workforce — Build organisational capability during rapid growth through a new Enterprise Capability Architecture, Skills and Competency Framework, talent acquisition and strategic workforce planning.

Reform & change drivers

  • AML/CTF Amendment Act 2024 (Royal Assent 10 Dec 2024) expanding the regime to Tranche 2 sectors from 1 July 2026, lifting regulated entities from ~19,000 to over 100,000
  • FATF comprehensive mutual evaluation across 2025-2027, requiring AUSTRAC to demonstrate effectiveness as both regulator and FIU
  • Establishment of a dedicated Data Division and Chief Data and Analytics Officer to lead data, analytics and AI uplift
  • Expansion of the EPMO and change management/service design functions to manage rapid reform and growth
  • Major ICT modernisation program (portal, CRM, contact centre, case management, corporate HR/finance/service-management platforms)
  • Comprehensive overhaul of performance measures with baselines to be established in 2025-26
  • Rapid workforce growth and the shift from a compliance-focused to an outcomes-focused regulatory model
  • Technological threat acceleration (AI, deepfakes, crypto/virtual assets) challenging identity verification and AML/CTF controls

Challenges & risks

  • Technology risk: agency technology may not meet future requirements, causing disruption and harm to reporting entities, partners and staff
  • People risk: insufficient resources and skilled staff during rapid growth, with culture and morale exposure
  • Regulation risk: ineffective industry engagement and management of regulatory interventions as the population grows fivefold
  • Information management, information security (including cyber) and data protection risk
  • Program and project management risk: inadequate governance leading to unrealised benefits and misused public monies
  • Onboarding ~80,000 small/low-complexity businesses without imposing unreasonable cost burden
  • New, unbaselined performance measures (most targets 'TBD' / 'establish a baseline') create evidence and methodology pressure ahead of FATF evaluation
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework constrains outsourcing of core work, limiting some advisory scope

In the news & controversies

  • Commentary argues AUSTRAC's strict-liability, enforcement-first model is poorly suited to the ~100,000 small businesses and 'gatekeeper' professions (lawyers, accountants, real estate agents, conveyancers, precious-metal dealers) entering the regime from 1 July 2026, contrasting it unfavourably with the more graduated, educational approaches of the ATO and ASIC. (Independent Australia, 'AUSTRAC tranche 2: Australia's toughest regulator to become its most hated?' — https://independentaustralia.net/business/business-display/austracs-tranche-2-australias-toughest-regulator-to-become-its-most-hated,20922)
    Signals demand for AUSTRAC to strengthen guidance, education and stakeholder engagement to soften a punitive image, and a large external readiness market — but for AUSTRAC itself the BD angle is supporting its industry-facing engagement, guidance design and Tranche 2 onboarding capability.

Caveats: This brief is grounded in a clear, signal-rich plan, but several factors temper confidence. AUSTRAC explicitly operates under the Strategic Commissioning Framework and states core work is done in-house with only minimal, narrowly-permitted outsourcing — this constrains advisory scope and means the strongest fits are time-bound reform delivery, assurance, digital build and data work rather than ongoing core functions. As a security/intelligence-adjacent agency, clearance and access constraints will apply to FIU-related work.
