Civil Aviation Safety Authority (CASA)
Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications, Sport & Arts · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-26 to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Medium

CASA is an independent statutory (corporate Commonwealth) entity and aviation safety regulator funded by an aviation fuel excise, government appropriation, and regulatory fees/levies. The plan is dominated by two pressures: a structurally challenged funding model (budgeted operating deficits of $14.7m in 2025-26 widening to ~$20m, drawing down reserves, with fuel-excise revenue threatened by fleet electrification and fuel-efficiency shifts), and rapid industry change (RPAS/drones now exceed flight-crew licences, advanced air mobility/eVTOL certification, Western Sydney Airport opening 2026, and the new Australian Future Airspace Framework). CASA is mid-stream on a multi-year flight-operations and airworthiness regulatory transition, implementing a National Oversight Plan to move to risk-based surveillance (responding to ANAO recommendations), expanding service-delivery transparency dashboards, and modernising core systems (Enterprise Aviation Processing, myCASA, Medical Records System) under a Digital Business Strategy with Essential Eight cyber uplift. As a small, expertise-heavy regulator with admitted financial-sustainability, workforce and digital pressures, the best-fit demand sits in Financial & Accounting, Digital & ICT Delivery, Cybersecurity, Data/Analytics and assurance/governance — not large delivery programs.

Strategic priorities

  • Flight operations & airworthiness regulatory transition — Continue the multi-year transition to the flight operations regulations (introduced 2021) and reform of airworthiness rules, including new CASR Part 43, Part 92 dangerous goods, Part 66 engineer licensing, and Parts 103/131. CASA explicitly states the legislative and industry transition will continue for several years.
  • Integrating emerging aviation technologies — Support safe integration of RPAS/drones, advanced air mobility (eVTOL, e.g. Joby JAS4-1 type certification), uncrewed traffic management (UTM), vertiports, and consult on the Australian Future Airspace Framework (AFAF). Drone licence holders now exceed flight-crew licences.
  • Service-delivery transparency and timeliness — Expand the online service-delivery dashboard, deliver against the September 2024 service charter, establish published timeframes for 75% of regulatory services in 2025-26 (100% by 2026-27) and meet timeframes 80% of the time. CASA admits the timeframe-development target has not been achieved since 2019-20.
  • National Oversight Plan / risk-based surveillance — Implement the National Oversight Plan to consolidate oversight into a risk-based surveillance program, explicitly aligned to ANAO recommendations and ICAO critical elements, with improved data capture and systems-level trend analysis.
  • Digital modernisation and cyber uplift — Continue the Digital Business Strategy 2023-2026: invest $7.3m capital in 2025-26 (and further in forward years) in the Enterprise Aviation Processing (EAP) system, myCASA portal, Medical Records System and network operations, while lifting cyber maturity to Essential Eight and modernising gateway security.
  • Workforce capability and culture — Implement the Strategic Workforce Plan 2024-2027 and People and Culture Strategy 2022-2027 to attract/retain a dispersed, highly professional workforce amid industry-wide skills shortages (engineers, pilots) and build skills for emerging-technology regulation.

Reform & change drivers

  • Aviation White Paper and revised whole-of-government Regulatory Policy, Practice and Performance Framework giving CASA responsibility for safe rollout of new technologies and reducing GA regulatory burden
  • Ministerial Statement of Expectations (2023-25) driving surveillance, transparency, service-delivery and regulatory-program obligations
  • Rapid sector growth: 46% increase in authorisation holders and ~50% increase in pilot licences over five years; RPAS licence holders now exceed flight-crew licences
  • Major infrastructure changes: Western Sydney International Airport opening 2026, new runways at Perth and Melbourne
  • Emerging technology: advanced air mobility/eVTOL certification, UTM Action Plan, vertiports, MOSAIC airworthiness rule-making, space-launch activity
  • Funding-model fragility: fuel-excise revenue (3.556 cents/litre) threatened by fuel-efficiency gains and electrification; RPAS/aerodrome/international-carrier oversight does not contribute to excise
  • ANAO recommendations driving the move to risk-based surveillance via the National Oversight Plan
  • ICAO obligations including USOAP Continuous Monitoring Approach

Challenges & risks

  • Budgeted operating deficits every year of the plan ($14.7m, $20.5m, $16.7m, $16.7m), funded by drawing down retained surpluses, cash and investment reserves — an explicitly unsustainable trajectory
  • Strategic risk that CASA 'fails to maintain positive financial stability' given uncertainty over the medium-to-long-term funding model
  • Cybersecurity strategic risk (appetite 'Small') with an active program to reach Essential Eight and modernise gateway security
  • Workforce/skills shortages (licensed engineers, pilots) affecting both industry and CASA's own capability to regulate emerging technologies
  • Persistent failure to establish regulatory service-delivery timeframes (target unmet since 2019-20) despite it now being a headline transparency commitment
  • Many performance targets are newly introduced or 'same as 2025-26' with qualitative 'trend' measures, and some past results 'could not be confirmed due to issues with data currency'
  • Ageing/under-development core systems (EAP, myCASA, Medical Records System) requiring sustained capital while budgets are in deficit
  • Strategic risk that CASA is 'unable to adapt to the evolving aviation environment' given the pace of RPAS/AAM change
