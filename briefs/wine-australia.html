Wine Australia is a small corporate Commonwealth entity (a Rural Research & Development Corporation) funded by grower/winemaker/exporter levies plus Commonwealth matching for R&D, with three core activities: Research, Innovation & Adoption; Market Development; and Regulatory Services. It is operating in a sector in acute financial distress (oversupply, flat levies) and is responding through the 2024 One Grape & Wine Sector Plan and a newly created Australian Wine Future Fund (with a Research & Innovation Fund and a Venture Investments Portfolio), enabled by July 2025 legislative changes that let it pursue non-levy funding. New build/initiatives — the National Vineyard Register and the Future Fund — plus a known ANAO probity focus on RDCs create credible, proportionate demand for investment-governance, program delivery, probity, data/ICT and financial-sustainability advisory. Keep all offerings sized to a small statutory authority under levy/budget pressure.

Strategic priorities

  • Stand up the Australian Wine Future Fund — Operationalise a new investment vehicle with two streams — a Research & Innovation Fund and a Venture Investments Portfolio — to unlock non-levy investment while levies remain flat, leveraging increased Commonwealth matching from July 2025.
  • Deliver against the One Grape & Wine Sector Plan — Coordinate sector-wide priorities and actions to 2030 to improve profitability and resilience and align plans across sector organisations toward Vision 2050.
  • Build the National Vineyard Register — Create a planned national register/database of all grapevine plantings to help balance supply and demand and support sector initiatives.
  • Grow demand and premium through Market Development — Increase demand and the premium paid for Australian wine across markets, including new domestic campaigns ('We make a wine for that') and emerging categories such as mid-strength/lighter lifestyle wines and export-market diversification (Canada, USA).
  • Safeguard product integrity via Regulatory Services — Maintain export and compliance regulation (product approvals, VI-1 certificates, shipping approvals, export licences) to uphold the sector's reputation and market confidence.

Reform & change drivers

  • 2024 One Grape & Wine Sector Plan setting priorities and actions to 2030
  • Legislative change from July 2025 increasing Commonwealth R&D matching and enabling Wine Australia to seek non-levy funding
  • Creation of the Australian Wine Future Fund, including a Venture Investments Portfolio (a non-traditional activity for an RDC)
  • Flat levy revenue against a backdrop of sector-wide oversupply and grower financial distress
  • Shifting global consumer preferences toward mid-strength / lighter lifestyle and NOLO wines
  • Export market diversification following past trade disruption (Canada, USA momentum)
  • Ethical/labour-exploitation risk management (Ethical Business Guide) and Reflect Reconciliation Action Plan commitments

Challenges & risks

  • Structural oversupply and grower distress increasing scrutiny over how levy funds are deployed
  • Flat levy income limiting investment capacity, driving reliance on matching funds and new non-levy revenue
  • Establishing investment-governance and probity controls for a Venture Investments Portfolio — a higher-risk activity than traditional grant-style R&D funding
  • Delivery and data-integrity risk in building the National Vineyard Register
  • ANAO-identified probity focus across Rural RDCs (conflicts of interest, grants/contract management)
  • Reputational sensitivity: any consulting/admin spend in a distressed-sector context invites political and grower criticism
  • Wine Australia's narrow, non-advocacy remit creates dependence on partners (e.g. Australian Grape & Wine) and government during sector crises

    The levy-paying base is in acute distress, sharpening scrutiny of how funds and any restructuring/support are deployed — demand for strategy, evaluation and stakeholder-engagement work, but with high reputational sensitivity around spend.
  • Commentary notes a Senate committee found the Wine Equalisation Tax (WET) rebate distorts the industry and is subject to widespread 'rorting' — a Treasury tax-policy issue affecting the sector, not conduct attributed to Wine Australia. (The Conversation, 'Australia's reputation for fine wines is under threat' — https://theconversation.com/australias-reputation-for-fine-wines-is-under-threat-62432)
    Tax/levy grievances colour grower sentiment toward all sector institutions; scope work to Wine Australia's actual remit (R&D, marketing, export regulation), not WET policy.
