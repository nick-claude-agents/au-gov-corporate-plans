Austrade (Australian Trade and Investment Commission)
Foreign Affairs and Trade · Corporate Plan 2024-25 (with outlook to 2027-28) · BD confidence: Medium

Austrade is a globally-networked trade, investment and tourism promotion agency (100 locations, 61 offshore) that also delivers consular and passport services. For 2024-25 it has streamlined post-pandemic and is focused on a defined reform agenda: the EMDG Strategic Refocus and new grant round, trade modernisation/simplified-trade reforms, expanding the digital Go Global Toolkit, standing up Southeast Asia Investment Deal Teams and Technology Landing Pads, developing THRIVE 2030 Phase 2, and embedding evaluation and economic-impact disciplines. A critical BD constraint is explicit: the plan states all core work is done in-house and any consultant use must comply with the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework — so the realistic opportunities sit in enabling/corporate functions (digital, grants administration, evaluation/performance, governance and assurance) rather than the agency's frontline trade/investment work. Demand signals are real but modest in scale; this is a mid-sized agency with a long-running value-for-money narrative.

Strategic priorities

  • EMDG Strategic Refocus and new grant round — Finalise the redesign of the Export Market Development Grant program and open a new round with improved grant sizes, revised eligibility tied to exporter success, better sustainability and tighter integration with Austrade's other services.
  • Trade modernisation and digital trade services — Coordinate simplified trade system reforms (streamlining cross-border regulation and government processes) and expand the digital Go Global Toolkit with personalisation features to reach more exporters across metro, regional and remote Australia.
  • Investment facilitation for Future Made in Australia / net zero — Attract large-scale FDI aligned to sovereign capability (critical minerals, clean energy, batteries, defence, space) and stand up Southeast Asia Investment Deal Teams, Technology Landing Pads and regional deal-team hubs.
  • THRIVE 2030 Phase 2 for the visitor economy — Lead national visitor-economy policy and develop a Phase 2 Action Plan with refreshed targets (toward $230bn by 2030), including a new National Sustainability Framework and the First Nations Visitor Economy Partnership.
  • Embedding evaluation and economic-impact discipline — Build evaluation capability across Austrade to improve monitoring and accountability of programs and demonstrate economic impact — a direct response to the agency's need to prove ROI.
  • Technology and corporate-process uplift — Upgrade global network performance, simplify digital communication channels, and run projects to simplify corporate service processes and systems while meeting cyber and information-management requirements.

Reform & change drivers

  • Government priorities the agency must operationalise: diversification, net zero / Future Made in Australia, economic security, First Nations, visitor economy, and trade modernisation.
  • EMDG program redesign requiring new grant guidelines, eligibility rules and assessment processes.
  • Simplified trade system reform agenda involving cross-government regulatory, process and digital change.
  • Implementation of ASEAN-Australia Special Summit outcomes and the Southeast Asia Economic Strategy to 2040 (Deal Teams, Landing Pads).
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework constraining external consultant use and pushing core work in-house.
  • APS Net Zero 2030 targets requiring reductions in office energy, waste, water and digital-technology emissions.
  • Recovery of the visitor economy and development of THRIVE 2030 Phase 2 with new sustainability framework.

Challenges & risks

  • Stated enterprise risk that governance, business continuity, planning, risk, financial management and staff capability may not be fit for purpose.
  • Stated risk around balancing digital tools while meeting mandatory cyber-security and information-management requirements.
  • Stated risk of failing to identify unethical or illegal conduct across operations (integrity/fraud).
  • Performance measures are largely high-level and qualitative (e.g. 'effective contribution', topic lists), with policy focus areas 'to be determined' — a weak performance-measurement posture under PGPA.
  • Long-running external pressure to demonstrate value-for-money/ROI on the agency's budget.
  • Investment-outcome target lowered from 120 to 110, signalling resourcing trade-offs toward strategic priorities.
  • Strong in-house delivery culture and Strategic Commissioning Framework limit appetite for external consulting.
