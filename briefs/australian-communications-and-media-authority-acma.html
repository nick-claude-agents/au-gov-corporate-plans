Australian Communications and Media Authority (ACMA), including the eSafety Commissioner
Infrastructure, Transport, Regional Development, Communications, Sport & Arts · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-26 to 2028-29) · BD confidence: Medium

ACMA is an expertise-heavy communications and media regulator whose remit is expanding fast — spectrum modernisation, scam/SMS sender-ID controls, telco consumer protections, and a wave of legislative-instrument remakes. Its attached eSafety Commissioner is the higher-growth, higher-pressure component: it is implementing the world-watched Social Media Minimum Age regime (effective mid-Dec 2025), running Phase 2 industry codes/standards, awaiting government response to a 67-recommendation Online Safety Act review (including a digital duty of care), and scaling data, AI and regulatory technology. Both bodies are building new digital platforms (a digital spectrum management platform by 2026; a Sender ID Registry; SMMA enabling technology) and refreshing data, cyber and workforce capability. A critical BD headwind: ACMA explicitly commits to the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework and is targeting reduced outsourcing of administration, data/research and ICT/digital work — so advisory framing must emphasise specialist capability and surge delivery the agency cannot resource in-house. The collapsed misinformation bill and a departmental functional review signal an unsettled regulatory-design agenda.

Strategic priorities

  • Modernise spectrum and numbering systems — Deliver a new end-to-end digital spectrum management platform (online by 2026), maintain SPECTRA-based licensing, and prepare for WRC-2027 and demand for mid-band spectrum, D2D/LEOsat and 5G/6G.
  • Scam, spam and consumer-protection enforcement — Build and operate a Sender ID system to allow positive identification of SMS senders (in service December 2025), enforce new telco rules including domestic/family violence support and mobile number fraud.
  • eSafety: implement the Social Media Minimum Age regime — Stand up enabling technology, guidance on 'reasonable steps', evidence/evaluation (with a global Academic Advisory Group) and enforcement for the under-16 social media account ban effective mid-December 2025.
  • eSafety: industry codes/standards and duty of care — Oversee Phase 2 industry codes/standards (class 2 material), Basic Online Safety Expectations transparency, and prepare for a legislated digital duty of care following the Online Safety Act review's 67 recommendations.
  • Data-driven regulation and responsible AI — Grow data maturity (centralised Data Platform, catalogues, advanced/geospatial analytics) and embed AI governance across both ACMA and eSafety to regulate more efficiently and understand AI use by regulated entities.
  • Cost recovery and charging reform — Implement new cost recovery arrangements (including for the Sender ID Registry) and refine cost-attribution efficiency under the Australian Government Charging Framework.

Reform & change drivers

  • Online Safety Act independent review (Rickard, Oct 2024) — 67 recommendations including a digital duty of care, awaiting government response
  • Online Safety Amendment (Social Media Minimum Age) Act 2024 — mandatory under-16 account restriction effective mid-December 2025
  • Phase 2 industry codes/standards for class 2 material; penalties for breaches raised to nearly $50m
  • Universal Outdoor Mobile Obligation (UOMO) and Universal Service Obligation reform
  • Statement of Expectations (Dec 2024) and ACMA Statement of Intent (Mar 2025) emphasising proactive, risk-based regulation
  • Approximately 25 legislative instruments sunsetting in 2025-26 requiring review, consultation and remaking
  • WRC-2027 spectrum preparation and harmonisation; D2D/LEOsat regulatory adaptation
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework — targeted reductions in outsourcing of admin, data/research and ICT/digital work
  • Whole-of-government AI safety/ethics agenda and National Anti-Corruption Commission / APS Integrity Action Plan
  • New cost recovery arrangements including the Sender ID Registry

Challenges & risks

  • Named enterprise risk: inadequate resources to meet objectives, with eSafety explicitly citing 'insufficient resources to meet increasing regulatory responsibilities'
  • Named enterprise risk: loss or degradation of critical ICT infrastructure — driving cyber and disaster-recovery investment
  • Named enterprise risk: reduction in public confidence in regulated communications/media services
  • Delivery risk on multiple concurrent platform builds (spectrum platform, Sender ID, SMMA enabling tech) under tight statutory deadlines
  • Psychosocial/vicarious-trauma risk to eSafety staff handling distressing content
  • Reliance on outsourced operators (ZOAK numbering, Dataworks NSER, IVE DNCR) with availability-based performance measures
  • Unsettled regulatory-design agenda after the collapse of the misinformation/disinformation bill
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework constrains the type of work that can be outsourced — narrowing some advisory opportunities

In the news & controversies

  • The Combatting Misinformation and Disinformation Bill 2024, which would have substantially expanded ACMA's platform-oversight, code-approval and standard-making powers, was introduced in September 2024 and abandoned in November 2024 amid free-speech and 'government overreach' concerns. (Griffith Law Review (Taylor & Francis) https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10383441.2025.2522564 ; SBS News https://www.sbs.com.au/news/article/what-is-the-misinformation-bill-and-why-has-it-triggered-worries-about-freedom-of-speech/4n3ijebde)
    ACMA's digital-platform regulatory expansion is stalled and will likely be reset — creating latent demand for regulatory/policy redesign, proportionality and stakeholder-consultation support if the agenda is revived.
  • The Department of Infrastructure is running a review of ACMA's legislated functions, mapping its statutory powers across the Telecommunications Act 1997, Broadcasting Services Act 1992 and ACMA Act 2005. (Department of Infrastructure https://www.infrastructure.gov.au/media-technology-communications/media-laws-regulation/acma-legislated-functions)
    A live functional review often precedes operating-model or remit change — an opening for operating-model, governance and legislative-mapping advisory work.
  • ACMA's 2024-25 annual report records more than $12m in Spam Act penalties (including $7.5m against Commonwealth Bank, $4m Tabcorp, $1.6m Telstra for ID-check failures) and 1,200+ illegal offshore gambling sites blocked since 2019. (ACMA & eSafety Commissioner Annual Report 2024-25 https://www.esafety.gov.au/sites/default/files/2025-10/ACMA-eSafety-annual-report-2024-25.pdf)
    ACMA is in an active enforcement phase across spam, gambling and telco ID/scam compliance — signalling appetite for compliance-monitoring, investigations and data-handling capability.

Parliamentary matters

  • The Combatting Misinformation and Disinformation Bill 2024, centred on expanded ACMA powers, was the subject of Senate committee scrutiny, with evidence taken before the Bill was abandoned. (Human Rights Law Centre https://www.hrlc.org.au/submissions/2024/11/8/submission-combatting-misinformation ; Australian Human Rights Commission https://humanrights.gov.au/our-work/legal/submission/combatting-misinformation-and-disinformation-bill-2024)
    Parliamentary processes around ACMA's remit are an active arena; regulatory-impact, evidence-base and consultation support are plausible engagement points when the agenda re-emerges.

Caveats: This is a content-rich plan and signals are well evidenced, but two structural caveats temper the opportunity. First, ACMA explicitly commits to the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework and is targeting reduced outsourcing of administration, data/research and ICT/digital work — a direct headwind for several mappings, so offerings must be framed as specialist/surge capability rather than routine resourcing. Second, several of the largest opportunities (duty of care, functional-review operating model, revived platform-regulation agenda) depend on government decisions not yet made, so timing is uncertain. ANAO research was incomplete due to tool rate limits — absence of ANAO findings here is not confirmation none exist, and a follow-up search is recommended. Budgets are modest for a regulator (eSafety ~$71.7m, 242 staff in 2025-26), so engagements should be right-sized. No individuals beyond named office-holders should be approached cold.
