Housing Australia
Treasury · Corporate Plan 2024-25 · BD confidence: High

Housing Australia is a corporate Commonwealth entity (a 'specialist investment vehicle' in the Treasury portfolio) that has roughly doubled in size in a single year (79 to 138 staff, heading to ~220) as it absorbed major new financing programs — the Housing Australia Future Fund Facility (HAFFF), National Housing Accord Facility (NHAF), an expanded National Housing Infrastructure Facility (NHIF) and the Home Guarantee Scheme. It is standing up programs, systems, risk frameworks and a workforce at speed, with more change pending (Help to Buy shared equity scheme, the extra $1bn NHIF for crisis/transitional accommodation). The plan openly flags reliance on contractors/consultants during establishment, immature outcomes-measurement methodology, a strengthening-but-evolving risk framework, and new technology/data builds. External scrutiny of HAFF delivery, cost and delay — including live ANAO performance audits and Senate inspection — sharpens demand for independent delivery assurance, program management, evaluation and financial/governance uplift. This is a high-growth, high-scrutiny financing body: strong fit for Programs/Change, Audit & Assurance, Financial & Accounting, Data & Analytics and Workforce work, sized to a lean (~200-person) organisation.

Strategic priorities

  • Stand up and scale the HAFFF and NHAF delivery — Deliver Government dwelling targets — 30,000 social/affordable under HAFFF and 10,000 affordable under NHAF over five years — through new funding documentation, evaluation panels and approval processes built at speed in 2023-24.
  • Establish processes for the expanded NHIF (crisis/transitional accommodation) — Build new funding documentation and processes to deliver the additional NHIF funding targeted at crisis and transitional accommodation for women and children fleeing domestic violence, and youth, working with DSS.
  • Prepare for new programs subject to legislation (Help to Buy, NHIF expansion) — Position to deliver the Help to Buy shared equity scheme (a first-of-type Commonwealth program) and the $1bn NHIF expansion once legislation and Investment Mandate changes pass.
  • Mature outcomes measurement and evaluation — Refine methodologies to demonstrate program impact, value for money and additionality, and establish best-practice evaluation frameworks and reports after each funding round.
  • Build internal capability and a sustainable operating model — Replace contractors/consultants with permanent staff, review the operating model around Origination, Credit, Treasury and Finance, and embed culture as the organisation grows to ~220 staff.
  • Strengthen risk, technology and data foundations — Mature the enterprise risk framework (three lines of defence), bring IT fully in-house from Export Finance Australia, build customer-facing/digitised systems, and strengthen the data governance framework.

Reform & change drivers

  • Major legislative expansion (Housing Australia Future Fund Act 2023, National Housing Supply and Affordability Council Act, Treasury Laws Amendment (Housing Measures No. 1) Act) that created HAFFF and NHAF responsibilities
  • Pending Help to Buy shared equity scheme legislation (before the Senate) and a pending Investment Mandate amendment for the additional $1bn NHIF
  • 2024-25 Budget changes: NHIF liability cap raised to $10bn, Line of Credit to $4bn, NHIF redirected to crisis/transitional accommodation, Capacity Building Program doubled to $3m
  • Department of Finance establishing a central oversight/governance function for Specialist Investment Vehicles, increasing accountability expectations on balance-sheet risk
  • Government expectation that key functions be delivered by internal staff rather than contractors/consultants
  • Macroeconomic pressure: construction-sector cost inflation (~40% since 2019), insolvencies, tight rental markets, and capital-market funding costs that constrain delivery

Challenges & risks

  • Rapid organisational growth straining operating model, systems and workforce; transition from heavy contractor/consultant reliance to permanent staffing with knowledge-transfer risk
  • Capacity and capability of the community housing sector to absorb funding and actually deliver supply (concentration in Tier 1/Tier 2 CHPs)
  • Delivery risk on first-of-type and time-bound programs (Help to Buy, crisis accommodation NHIF) against fixed Government dwelling targets
  • Immature outcomes/impact measurement methodology and newly introduced performance measures (rationalised from 35 to 14, several with 'Yes/No' targets)
  • Credit, financial, market and operational risk as lending, credit and portfolio-management activity grows in complexity
  • Cyber and data-governance exposure as the technology footprint and data sets expand and IT is brought in-house
  • Intense external scrutiny of HAFF delivery, cost and delay, including ANAO audits and parliamentary inquiries
