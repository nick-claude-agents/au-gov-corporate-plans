Australian Border Force (ABF)
Home Affairs · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

The corporate plan analysed is the Department of Home Affairs 2025-26 Corporate Plan, which encompasses the ABF as the portfolio's operational border agency (Outcome 3 in particular). The agency is mid-transformation following the May 2025 MoG changes (functions realigned 17 July 2025) and a formal response to the 2024 APSC Capability Review via a Transformation Action Plan, Workforce Strategy 2025-27, ABF Strategy 2025-27 and a 10-year Digital Investment Plan. Major time-bound delivery pressures dominate: standing up standalone border facilities at Western Sydney International Airport (opening 2026), the Simplified Trade System reform, preparing for the 2032 Brisbane Olympics, modernising ageing visa/trade/travel ICT, and sustaining maritime surveillance and Operation Sovereign Borders/Nauru enduring capability. The plan openly admits financial-management capability, ICT modernisation funding ('pending investment'), cyber resilience and workforce/culture risks. This is a large service-delivery and enforcement agency with deep, multi-year demand for program delivery, ICT build, assurance, costing/financial sustainability and workforce/culture work — though clearance and security constraints will shape access.

Strategic priorities

  • Border modernisation — Western Sydney Airport & Simplified Trade System — Deliver standalone border facilities (including the Joint Cargo Examination Facility) at WSA ahead of its 2026 opening and progress the Simplified Trade System reform agenda (cargo intervention model, Digital Trade Accelerator, regulatory reform projects).
  • ICT and data modernisation via the 10-year Digital Investment Plan — Modernise critical visa, trade and travel ICT and operational technologies that control movement of people and goods at the border, improve resilience against cyber threats, and adopt AI and automation for productivity and service gains.
  • Transformation Agenda and culture/capability uplift — Shift from foundational actions to embedding long-term cultural and capability uplift across the department and ABF in response to the 2024 APSC Capability Review, through the Transformation Action Plan's three pillars.
  • Financial management capability and fiscal sustainability — Lift financial management capability, budget discipline and management accountability; finalise and fund a 10-year capital investment plan for enduring capability.
  • Civil maritime security and Operation Sovereign Borders enduring capability — Sustain aerial surveillance and Blue Water Fleet availability, support JATF OSB, and stand up an enduring regional processing capability with the Government of Nauru.
  • Migration and citizenship processing throughput — Deliver the Migration Program to planning levels, improve visa processing productivity, and dramatically reduce the on-hand citizenship caseload from 160,118 to 80,000.

Reform & change drivers

  • May 2025 machinery-of-Government changes and 17 July 2025 functional realignment, expanding portfolio collaboration
  • 2024 APSC Capability Review of Home Affairs and its Transformation Action Plan response
  • Western Sydney International Airport opening in 2026 and preparation for the 2032 Brisbane Olympics
  • Simplified Trade System microeconomic reform agenda requiring modernised ICT and streamlined operating models
  • 10-year Digital Investment Plan and Data and Digital Government Strategy driving ICT/data modernisation (pending funding)
  • Cyber Security Strategy 2023-2030 Horizon 1 completion and Horizon 2 implementation plan
  • Safer Australia Counter Terrorism and Violent Extremism Strategy 2025 and new National Support and Intervention Program (NSIP)
  • NZYQ High Court decision driving Bridging (Removal Pending) visa management and the Community Protection Board
  • PSPF assurance pilot, Essential Eight maturity uplift and National Anti-Corruption Commission obligations
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework requiring conversion of labour-hire contractors to APS staff (40 FTE target)
  • Citizenship caseload backlog requiring throughput uplift to over 300,000 decisions per annum

Challenges & risks

  • ICT modernisation explicitly contingent on funding ('pending investment') for critical visa, trade and travel systems
  • Financial management capability and fiscal sustainability identified as needing uplift; 10-year capital plan needs funding secured
  • Workforce risk: attracting/retaining capability, psychosocial risk across operational settings, and culture issues (Respect@ABF, AHRC recommendations)
  • Integrity and security risk including fraud/corruption, insider threat, and cyber-enabled misconduct
  • Cyber resilience and Essential Eight maturity gaps against high-impact attack threats to critical infrastructure
  • Organisational compliance risk given high-volume statutory decision-making exposed to legal challenge
  • Sustaining ageing maritime fleet and surveillance availability amid unscheduled maintenance and crew constraints
  • Large citizenship and protection-visa backlogs creating throughput pressure
  • Strategic Commissioning Framework reducing reliance on external labour hire — a headwind for contractor-style engagements
