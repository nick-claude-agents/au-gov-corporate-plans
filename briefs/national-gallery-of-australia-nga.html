The corporate plan supplied is the National Portrait Gallery's (NPG) 2025-26 plan, not the National Gallery of Australia's, despite the brief naming the NGA. The NPG is a small (~55 FTE) corporate Commonwealth cultural institution dedicated to portraiture, balancing government appropriation with own-source revenue (philanthropy, commercial, partnerships) and explicitly stating it must find efficiency savings or grow non-government revenue every year to stay in balance. Its deliberate agenda for the year is a national audience-engagement push (touring, digital and offsite reach beyond Canberra), continued digitisation and digital/AR/AI uplift, collection growth with a First Nations focus, and workforce/succession resilience. BD fit is real but proportionate to a small budget — strongest in financial sustainability, digital/data uplift, cyber assurance, and workforce planning. The externally supplied research relates to the NGA (a separate entity with a well-publicised leaky-building funding crisis), so those signals should not be conflated with the NPG plan analysed here.

Strategic priorities

  • Enrich the collection (First Nations–led) — Grow the National Portrait Collection through acquisitions and commissions, elevating First Nations voices, and make works accessible through digital content, accessibility resources and interactive/AR experiences.
  • Inspire a national audience — Increase access beyond Canberra through new touring models, partnerships with peer national cultural institutions, continued digitisation (target 98% collection digitised) and digital learning, after reduced interstate visitation in 2024-25 underlined the need for a national engagement strategy.
  • Create connection (philanthropy & partnerships) — Grow private giving to the Foundation and maintain high-value commercial partnerships to diversify revenue and build financial resilience.
  • Financial sustainability — Maintain budget balance through ongoing efficiency savings or growth in non-government revenue every year, in an environment of rising operating costs and constrained corporate/private support.

Reform & change drivers

  • National Cultural Policy 'Revive' and the Minister's 2024 Statement of Expectations driving the five-pillar agenda (First Nations first, a place for every story, centrality of the artist, strong cultural infrastructure, engaging the audience)
  • Shift to national/offsite reach via new touring models after reduced interstate visitation to Canberra
  • Digital uplift: continued investment in digital infrastructure and personnel, AR onsite activations, and active exploration of AI (e.g. Portrait Stories app, 'Mission to Mars' interactive)
  • Financial model dependent on growing own-source revenue (philanthropy, commercial partnerships, Foundation investment earnings) amid rising operating costs
  • Workforce pressure: succession planning for critical roles and competition for talent, particularly First Nations talent
  • Lifecycle capital works program to maintain the building, restored by 2023-24 Budget funding

Challenges & risks

  • Funding and resources risk: insufficient funds to fulfil legislative function or strategic priorities due to rising operating costs and constrained private/government support
  • Reputation risk from confronting/challenging content or gaps in governance practices (donor and curatorial-independence sensitivities)
  • Physical infrastructure risk including cyber-attack, environmental events and disruptions affecting audience access
  • Collection damage from accidental/deliberate interference, transport, installation or environmental conditions
  • Workforce risk: inability to attract/retain talent, particularly First Nations talent, and capacity/capability gaps
  • Cyber security: acknowledged increased threat environment requiring ongoing penetration/intrusion testing
