Australian Institute of Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Studies (AIATSIS)
Prime Minister and Cabinet · Corporate Plan 2025-2029 (budget year 2025-26 detailed) · BD confidence: Medium

AIATSIS is a small, corporate Commonwealth entity and the only National Cultural Institution dedicated to First Nations cultures, operating as a custodial/collecting institution under the AIATSIS Act 1989. Its dominant near-term agenda is the Ngurra National Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander Cultural Precinct — a major capital build comprising a National Indigenous Knowledge and Cultural Centre and a National Resting Place (a Revive policy commitment) — alongside large-scale collection digitisation (against a UNESCO 2025 audio-visual obsolescence deadline) and delivery of its Digital Strategy 2021-2030. The plan candidly flags financial sustainability pressure, the need to diversify funding, workforce attraction/retention competition, immature/growing risk and governance frameworks, and an expanding staffing profile. For the Consulting Firm, the strongest, most time-bound demand sits in program delivery and assurance of Ngurra, financial-sustainability and costing work, digitisation/ICT delivery, and governance/risk framework uplift — all proportionate to a modestly-resourced institution.

Strategic priorities

  • Deliver the Ngurra Cultural Precinct (incl. National Resting Place) — Build a National Indigenous Knowledge and Cultural Centre (extensions to the Maraga building) and a National Resting Place for repatriated ancestral remains — a major multi-year capital and program undertaking and a Commonwealth commitment under the Revive National Cultural Policy.
  • Preserve and digitise the national collection — Grow, preserve, store and digitise the world's largest collection of its kind, including the Central Australian Digitisation Facility and On-Country Digitisation Initiative, against archival standards and the UNESCO 2025 audio-visual obsolescence deadline.
  • Modernise ICT and implement the Digital Strategy — Modernise and integrate the ICT environment to improve knowledge management and discoverability of the collection, and implement the AIATSIS Digital Strategy 2021-2030 including collection management infrastructure upgrades.
  • Financial sustainability and funding diversification — Respond to a fiscally challenging environment by developing a multi-channel funding approach (government grants, philanthropy, corporate sponsorship, revenue-generating activity), implementing cost-efficient practices and increasing financial literacy of staff and Council.
  • Repatriation / Return of Cultural Heritage — Facilitate the return of ancestral remains and cultural heritage material from overseas institutions, private collectors and foreign governments, including temporary custodianship and return to Traditional Custodians.
  • Strengthen governance, risk and workforce capability — Develop risk management practices and revise internal governance frameworks to accommodate growth, develop a workforce plan addressing capability gaps, strengthen leadership and increase Indigenous representation.

Reform & change drivers

  • Revive (National Cultural Policy) commitment underpinning the National Resting Place and Ngurra precinct
  • UNESCO 2025 deadline on audio-visual obsolescence forcing urgency on digitisation
  • AIATSIS Digital Strategy 2021-2030 driving ICT modernisation and collection-management infrastructure upgrade
  • Growing demand and expectation for AIATSIS expertise, collections and advisory services
  • Expanding staffing profile and operating model as the institution grows toward Ngurra
  • Indigenous Data Sovereignty and governance principles shaping digital research infrastructure
  • Fiscally constrained environment requiring funding diversification beyond appropriations
  • National truth-telling process and reporting on the situation and status of Indigenous cultures and heritage

Challenges & risks

  • Financial sustainability — explicitly named 'fiscally challenging environment' and risk that systems, policies and processes are not sustainable or fit for purpose
  • Failure to grow, preserve, manage and store physical and digital collections and data assets
  • Audio-visual obsolescence pressing against the UNESCO 2025 deadline
  • Workforce attraction and retention under public-sector salary competition, with focus on Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander staff
  • Internal operating environment change as staffing grows to reflect expansion — governance frameworks needing revision
  • Developing/immature risk management practices being strengthened
  • Managing multiple specialised areas of professional expertise in a dynamic environment
  • Delivering a major capital precinct (Ngurra) — a large program relative to the institution's size and capability
  • Acknowledged performance-measure limitations (data source/system constraints on licences and Code of Ethics downloads)
