Department of Education
Education · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

The Department of Education is a policy and stewardship department running a heavy reform agenda in 2025-26 across all three education sectors: early childhood (Building Early Education Fund, Three Day Guarantee, Worker Retention Payment, Child Care Subsidy changes), schools (Better and Fairer Schools Agreements 2025-2034), and higher education (standing up the Australian Tertiary Education Commission, a new equity funding system, 20% HELP reduction, Commonwealth Prac Payment, and contested international-education integrity reforms). It is simultaneously responding to a 2025 APS Capability Review (action plan due August 2025), refreshing its People, Data, ICT, Finance and Evaluation strategies, and building in-house policy, data and evaluation capability under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework. Notable external pressure points are an ANAO 'partly effective' finding on Child Care Subsidy compliance ($13.6bn program), a contested Senate inquiry into the ESOS international-student cap bill, and a flagged departmental redundancy round under the Government's savings/consultant-spend scrutiny. Demand for the firm centres on program delivery and assurance of named reforms, evaluation, data, performance-framework and governance/regulatory-maturity work — tempered by the department's stated intent to insource core work and political sensitivity around consultant spend.

Strategic priorities

  • Establish the Australian Tertiary Education Commission (ATEC) — Stand up ATEC as steward of higher education in interim form from 1 July 2025 and permanently from 1 January 2026 (subject to legislation), led by three independent commissioners, with performance measures to be developed for 2026-27.
  • Early childhood education and care reform — Deliver the Building Early Education Fund (building more centres), the Three Day Guarantee and Worker Retention Payment, manage Child Care Subsidy changes, and strengthen quality and safety with state/territory regulators.
  • Better and Fairer Schools reforms — Implement the Better and Fairer Schools Agreement 2025-2034 and Full and Fair Funding agreement, with improvement measures tied to NAPLAN, attendance and Year 12/Cert III attainment targets, focused on equity cohorts.
  • International education integrity and sustainability — Implement a new framework to improve quality and integrity of international education and uphold sector sustainability, including a contested student-commencement cap regime via the ESOS Amendment Bill.
  • Higher education equity and access funding — Develop a new funding system to support under-represented students, implement the 20% HELP reduction, establish the Commonwealth Prac Payment, and expand regional/suburban university study hubs.
  • Respond to the 2025 Capability Review — Develop and publish (August 2025) an action plan responding to the department's APS Capability Review pilot, focusing on strategy, partnerships and user focus over four years.
  • Strengthen strategic policy, data and evaluation — Develop a long-term stewardship strategy, uplift in-house policy/data/economic analysis/evaluation capability, deliver the Evaluation Strategy 2025-2027 and Data Strategy, and mature performance reporting.
  • Support the Government's productivity agenda — Assist the Government's three-year productivity focus by helping build a skilled and adaptable workforce and bringing higher education and vocational training closer together.

In the news & controversies

  • The ESOS Amendment (Quality and Integrity) Bill 2024, capping new international student commencements (270,000 for 2025), drew sustained objection from universities and the ELICOS/VET sector, with criticism of the cap methodology and the concentration of cap-setting power with the Minister; the sector launched a petition and floated collective legal action. (ICEF Monitor, 3 Oct 2024 — https://monitor.icef.com/2024/10/criticism-mounts-as-australian-senate-committee-hears-it-is-wrong-to-go-ahead-with-esos-amendments-bill-as-is/)
    A high-stakes, contested reform creates demand for implementation support, sector consultation, regulatory design and economic-impact modelling — but carries reputational risk in being associated with a divisive policy.
  • The department is reported to be the next APS agency to offer redundancies as part of the Government's savings agenda; the Greens warned that hollowing out the public sector through redundancies would drive more spending on consultants and labour hire. (The Canberra Times (≈Nov 2025) — https://www.canberratimes.com.au/story/9207631/department-of-education-next-aps-agency-to-offer-redundancies/)
    Headcount cuts can open external-provider gaps, but heightened scrutiny on consultant/labour-hire value and the in-housing push means engagements must be tightly justified on value.
  • Following the OECD's 2024 Education at a Glance, the Australian Education Union argued Australia significantly underperforms most OECD countries on public-school investment while having the highest OECD private-school expenditure, and highlighted a First Nations attainment gap. (Australian Education Union media release, 10 Sep 2024 — https://www.aeufederal.org.au/news-media/media-releases/2024/september/new-oecd-report-highlights-inequity-australias-education-system)
    Persistent funding-equity debate keeps schools-funding policy, evaluation and equity programs on the agenda — a likely source of advisory and evaluation work. Note this is advocacy commentary, not a finding on departmental administration.

ANAO audit findings

  • ANAO Report No. 42 of 2024-25 found management and oversight of compliance activities within the $13.6bn Child Care Subsidy Program (Education and Services Australia) only 'partly effective', including that the Operational Committee responsible for shared risks does not consider shared compliance risks. (ANAO Auditor-General Report No. 42 2024-25 — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/performance-audit/management-and-oversight-of-compliance-activities-within-the-child-care-subsidy-program)
    A 'partly effective' rating on a major program is a strong, specific entry point for program-integrity/compliance design, fraud-risk, shared-services governance (the Education-Services Australia interface) and data-analytics work.
  • ANAO notes that of five 2024-25 performance audits examining regulatory activities, four reached 'negative' conclusions, frequently citing shortcomings in implementing risk-based approaches to regulation, with the CCS compliance audit as an example. (ANAO 2024-25 Performance Audit Outcomes — https://www.anao.gov.au/work/information/2024-25-performance-audit-outcomes)
    A broad ANAO theme of weak risk-based regulation suggests appetite and political pressure for regulatory-maturity, assurance and risk-framework work across the Education portfolio.

Parliamentary matters

  • The Senate Education and Employment Legislation Committee inquired into the ESOS Amendment (Quality and Integrity) Bill 2024, held additional hearings, faced cross-bench scepticism over the cap formula's purpose, and recommended removing course-level caps for public institutions while retaining institution-wide caps. (Senate Standing Committees on Education and Employment (via ICEF Monitor, 3 Oct 2024) — https://monitor.icef.com/2024/10/criticism-mounts-as-australian-senate-committee-hears-it-is-wrong-to-go-ahead-with-esos-amendments-bill-as-is/)
    An active legislative inquiry with contested policy detail (cap methodology, ministerial powers) creates demand for regulatory-impact analysis, sector engagement and design work, and signals the framework may still shift.
