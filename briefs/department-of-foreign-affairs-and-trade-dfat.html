Department of Foreign Affairs and Trade (DFAT)
Foreign Affairs and Trade · Corporate Plan 2025-26 · BD confidence: Medium

DFAT is a large, globally distributed policy and service-delivery department operating 115 posts across 86 countries in an environment it describes as more contested and unstable than ever. For 2025-26 it is pursuing workforce capability uplift under the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework (having cut its contracted workforce 17% since 2023), a continued ICT/AI modernisation agenda, regulatory maturity across sanctions and foreign arrangements, and management of a substantial overseas property estate and security/cyber footprint. It also runs an ~1,000-investment development program and high-volume consular and passport services already subject to ANAO scrutiny. The Strategic Commissioning Framework's emphasis on insourcing tempers consulting demand, so the strongest, most defensible opportunities sit in specialised assurance, costing, digital/cyber delivery and property/program areas where internal APS capacity is genuinely constrained.

Strategic priorities

  • Workforce capability uplift and reduced contractor reliance — DFAT is implementing the APS Strategic Commissioning Framework, building internal APS capability through workforce planning, learning, streamlined recruitment and deployment, having already reduced its contracted workforce by 17% since 2023.
  • Secure, modern global ICT network and responsible AI — DFAT will continue enhancing its ICT capability over four years to deliver a reliable, secure, sustainable global network, including the transparent and safe use of AI to lift productivity.
  • Regulatory best practice across sanctions and foreign arrangements — DFAT is formalising regulator best-practice principles across three regulatory functions (ASNO, the Australian Sanctions Office and the Foreign Arrangements Branch), including a new 2025-26 performance measure on regulator effectiveness.
  • Overseas security and cyber resilience — DFAT is protecting government staff, information and assets overseas through risk-focused security measures and rolling out the Overseas Resilience to Cyber Activities program to 80% of risk-selected posts, with a new target added for ICT infrastructure at posts.
  • Overseas property estate sustainability and reinvestment — DFAT manages the Commonwealth's owned overseas property estate via the Overseas Property Office and a contracted service provider, committing to a minimum 2% annual reinvestment and 90% completion of preventative maintenance.
  • Consular and passport service performance — DFAT delivers high-volume passport and consular services with tightened efficiency targets, including a new fast-track passport service, following an ANAO efficiency audit.
  • Performance framework and APS performance statement maturity — DFAT commits to 13 performance measures (one new, five amended) and to improving performance reporting by incorporating lessons from ANAO audits of its annual performance statements.

Reform & change drivers

  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework driving insourcing and reduction of contracted workforce (down 17% since 2023)
  • Deteriorating geopolitical environment - authoritarianism, conflict, economic coercion and erosion of the rules-based trading system
  • Global reductions in development and humanitarian assistance forcing prioritisation and search for alternative delivery mechanisms
  • New regulator best-practice requirements (Finance RMG 128) and Statements of Expectations/Intent for sanctions, foreign arrangements and non-proliferation functions
  • ANAO audit of Australian Passport Office efficiency prompting revised passport performance measures and a new fast-track service
  • Adoption of AI and emerging technologies across a global, secure ICT network
  • AUKUS naval nuclear propulsion program creating new safeguards/verification obligations under the IAEA Comprehensive Safeguards Agreement
  • Ongoing maturation of enterprise risk reporting and performance-statement framework

Challenges & risks

  • Named enterprise risks: delivery, people, capability and capacity, enabling services, staff health/safety/wellbeing, financial management, and trust
  • Workforce capability and capacity gaps amid contractor reduction and reliance on the graduate pipeline and locally engaged staff
  • Maintaining a secure, sustainable global ICT network and managing cyber threats across 115 dispersed posts
  • Sustaining an ageing overseas property estate under fixed reinvestment targets and budget constraints
  • Operating in high-risk, volatile overseas environments that routinely exceed normal risk tolerance
  • Demand volatility in passport processing (5- and 10-year renewal cycles) complicating workforce planning
  • Integrity and conduct risks across a dispersed workforce including contractors and locally engaged staff
  • Measuring intangible diplomatic 'influence' credibly within the PGPA performance framework
