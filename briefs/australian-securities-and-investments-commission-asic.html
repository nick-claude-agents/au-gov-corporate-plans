ASIC is a Treasury-portfolio corporate regulator (expertise-heavy regulator archetype) whose 2025-26 Corporate Plan, released August 2025, is framed around responding to 'financial ecosystem threats' (media release 25-177MR). Critically, the supplied source is only the ASIC corporate-plan landing page — the substantive 2025-26 plan content was not in the text provided, so priority detail is inferred from the release title, ASIC's known mandate, and the external research. The dominant external signal is the July 2024 Senate inquiry that branded ASIC a 'failing' regulator and recommended structural reform, plus a live FRAA-effectiveness reform workstream with Treasury. This creates credible latent demand around regulatory effectiveness, governance uplift, data/intelligence capability, performance reporting and operating-model change — the natural fits for a regulator under reform pressure.

Strategic priorities

  • Responding to financial ecosystem threats — ASIC's 2025-26 plan is publicly positioned around responding to threats across the financial ecosystem — likely covering scams, digital/crypto assets, cyber-enabled misconduct, and emerging market risks. Exact priorities could not be confirmed from the supplied text.
  • Demonstrating enforcement and regulatory effectiveness — Following sustained criticism of its enforcement record, ASIC is under pressure to evidence effective investigation and enforcement outcomes and is working with Treasury on FRAA recommendations about its effectiveness.

Reform & change drivers

  • The July 2024 Senate Economics References Committee final report (11 recommendations) calling for structural reform, including potentially splitting ASIC into a companies regulator and a separate financial conduct authority
  • FRAA (Financial Regulator Assessment Authority) review of ASIC's effectiveness, with an active reform workstream involving Treasury
  • Recommendations for a new governance structure, legislated code of conduct, and whistleblower incentive/compensation arrangements
  • Growth in scams, digital-asset and cyber-enabled financial misconduct driving 'ecosystem threat' focus
  • Parliamentary accountability and transparency pressures, with the committee citing information being withheld

Challenges & risks

  • Reputational damage from being publicly characterised as a 'failing' regulator with an 'impossibly broad' remit
  • Structural uncertainty if government acts on the recommendation to split ASIC into two bodies
  • Pressure to lift investigation/enforcement throughput and the consistency of public reporting
  • Cultural and resourcing issues identified by the Senate inquiry
  • Regulatory technology and data/intelligence capability needed to detect and act on misconduct at scale

In the news & controversies

  • A 20-month Senate inquiry concluded in July 2024 that ASIC had 'comprehensively failed' its regulatory remit — taking no further action on most misconduct reports, investigating only a small fraction of complaints, and being too focused on internal problems to enforce the law. Coverage described it as a scathing assessment of the corporate regulator, citing an 'impossibly broad' remit and structural, resourcing and cultural issues. (Xinhua (english.news.cn/asiapacific/20240704/...); Global Legal Insights (globallegalinsights.com/news/australian-senate-inquiry-calls-for-asic-breakup/); QLS Proctor (qlsproctor.com.au/2024/07/senate-report-scathing-of-asic/))
    Strong demand signal: ASIC and Treasury face pressure to demonstrate enforcement reform, governance uplift, transparency/reporting consistency and operating-model change — fertile ground for advisory, capability and program-design work.
  • In response, ASIC said it would 'take time to consider' the findings, pointed to ~180 new investigations launched in 12 months, and said it was already working with Treasury on FRAA recommendations about its effectiveness. (Global Legal Insights (globallegalinsights.com/news/australian-senate-inquiry-calls-for-asic-breakup/))
    A live FRAA-linked reform workstream with Treasury indicates near-term engagements around effectiveness, data/reporting and governance — useful entry points.

Parliamentary matters

  • Senate Economics References Committee final report on ASIC investigation and enforcement (3 July 2024): referred October 2022, 200+ submissions, ~200-page report with 11 recommendations including splitting ASIC into two bodies, whistleblower incentives/compensation, a new governance structure and legislated code of conduct, and more funding. The committee said ASIC's parliamentary accountability was 'severely curtailed' by withheld information; a government response was due ~October 2024. (Senate Economics References Committee final report (transparencytaskforce.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/...); Murrays Legal summary (murrayslegal.com.au/blog/2024/07/04/...))
    Structural-separation, governance, code-of-conduct and whistleblower-program reform creates significant transformation, operating-model and change-management demand across ASIC and Treasury — high-value, multi-year potential.
  • ASIC senior officials gave evidence at Senate committee hearings, including Deputy Chair Sarah Court (Senate Economics Legislation Committee, 4 June 2024) and General Counsel Chris Savundra (Committee Hansard, 23 June 2024). (Senate Economics References Committee final report (transparencytaskforce.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/07/...))
    Estimates/Hansard exchanges surface live pain points and forward commitments; reviewing these transcripts can sharpen a tailored pitch.
