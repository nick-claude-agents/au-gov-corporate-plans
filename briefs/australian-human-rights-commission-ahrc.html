Australian Human Rights Commission (AHRC) — Business Development Brief

Generated 04 June 2026 · Source corporate plan
Australian Human Rights Commission (AHRC)
Attorney-General · Corporate Plan 2025-26 (covering 2025-2029) · BD confidence: Medium

The AHRC is a small, independent corporate Commonwealth entity and 'A-status' national human rights institution operating under acknowledged funding constraint while carrying an expanding mandate — most notably its newer regulator role for the positive duty under the Sex Discrimination Act. The plan is candid about capability gaps: it is replacing its case-management system with a new cloud-based ECMS, undertaking a SharePoint/PowerSuite uplift, launching a new website, investing in cyber security and process automation, and implementing recommendations from an ANAO performance audit of its complaints-handling function. It is also maturing its evaluation, workforce-planning and risk functions. BD demand is concentrated in enabling/corporate areas (digital, assurance, evaluation, workforce, costing) rather than large program delivery, and must be right-sized to a constrained budget. Live external pressures — funding/accreditation concerns, a complaints backlog, and a stalled Human Rights Act agenda — reinforce the corporate-uplift opportunities.

Strategic priorities

  • Reduce complaint-handling times and uplift the complaints function — With the backlog stabilised, the Commission is focused on reducing complaint-handling times and implementing ANAO recommendations to uplift complaints processes and infrastructure, underpinned by a new cloud-based ECMS.
  • Strengthen Australia's Human Rights Framework — Advocating for a National Human Rights Framework and Human Rights Act, discrimination-law reform (with a focus on disability discrimination), and the National Anti-Racism Framework.
  • Operate the positive-duty regulator function — Regulating the positive duty under the Sex Discrimination Act using risk-based, data-driven and best-practice regulatory approaches, ahead of an independent review of the function in 2026-27.
  • Financial sustainability and secure funding — Building on a 2025 capability review, the Commission is advocating for sustainable funding to preserve its independence and ability to deliver its mandate, including capital for cyber and automation.
  • Digital and information infrastructure modernisation — Migrating to 100% cloud, deploying a new ECMS, uplifting SharePoint/PowerSuite for automation, and launching a new accessible website by end 2025.
  • Build evaluation, MEAL and workforce capability — Maturing monitoring/evaluation/learning capability (participated in the Australian Centre for Evaluation maturity pilot) and expanding workforce planning, talent and job-family mapping.

Reform & change drivers

  • Free and Equal reforms — proposed Australian Human Rights Act, modernised anti-discrimination laws, and a national human rights education action plan
  • National Anti-Racism Framework and major racism research (universities study; Seen and Heard project)
  • New positive-duty regulator role under the Sex Discrimination Act, with an independent review of the function scheduled for 2026-27
  • Implementation of ANAO performance-audit recommendations on complaints handling
  • Sustained increase in FOI applications driving a new FOI policy and charging framework in late 2025
  • APS Strategic Commissioning Framework requiring in-house build of evaluation and graphic-design capability
  • Constrained funding environment and a 2025 capability review driving advocacy for sustainable funding
  • UN engagement milestones — Universal Periodic Review and Human Rights Council review in early 2026; GANHRI A-status accreditation maintenance

Challenges & risks

  • Working within a constrained funding environment as a named key enterprise risk, threatening delivery of mandate
  • Complaint-handling timeliness pressure (target 85% finalised under 12 months) and historical backlog
  • Cyber security incident response listed as a topical risk; reliance on manual processes pending automation
  • New regulatory function risks — regulatory capture, jurisdictional challenges, conflicting legislation, and stakeholder resistance
  • Workforce wellbeing and psychosocial/vicarious-trauma risk given the nature of the work
  • Maintaining A-status NHRI accreditation and reputation as a strong independent institution
  • Records/information management and physical/information security compliance obligations
  • Complex, long-horizon impact measurement across eight statutory officeholder portfolios with limited resources
